\section{The ARCP Pole Leg — P1 Fig.\,5}
\label{sec:P1Fig5}
% ============================================================

\figentry{fig:P1Fig5}{P1_Fig5_ARCP_pole_leg.png}
         {The Auxiliary Resonant Commutated Pole (ARCP) single phase leg.
          Source: De Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,3.}{0.75}

\begin{circuitbox}[Circuit Description — P1 Fig.\,5]
The ARCP pole leg consists of:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{DC bus}: split into two equal halves by two $2C_{dc}$
          capacitors, creating a midpoint rail at $\Vdc/2$
    \item \textbf{Main upper device}: $S_1$ (IGBT) with anti-parallel diode
          $D_1$; snubber capacitor $\Cr/2$ across $S_1$
    \item \textbf{Main lower device}: $S_2$ (IGBT) with anti-parallel diode
          $D_2$; snubber capacitor $\Cr/2$ across $S_2$
    \item \textbf{Pole node}: the junction of $S_1$ and $S_2$; voltage $\vf$
          swings between 0 and $\Vdc$ during commutation
    \item \textbf{Auxiliary switches}: $A_1$ and $A_2$ are back-to-back
          bidirectional switches (p-channel MCTs) connected between the
          \emph{DC bus midpoint} and the resonant inductor $\Lr$
    \item \textbf{Resonant inductor}: $\Lr$ connects from the $A_1$/$A_2$
          pair to the pole node; carries auxiliary current $\ir$
    \item \textbf{Load}: filter inductor $L_F$ and load current $I_F$ at
          the pole node; $V_F$ is the filtered output voltage
\end{itemize}
The auxiliary branch ($A_1$--$A_2$--$\Lr$) is \emph{only active during
commutation}. At steady state $\ir = 0$.
\end{circuitbox}

\begin{eqnbox}
\textbf{When do these equations apply? — the exact circuit condition.}

These equations are valid only during the brief interval when all of the
following are simultaneously true:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $A_1$ is \textbf{on} — the DC midpoint is connected through $\Lr$
          to the pole node, so $\Vdc/2$ drives the auxiliary inductor.
    \item $D_2$ has \textbf{just stopped conducting} — the pole node is no
          longer clamped to 0\,V and is free to rise.
    \item Both $\Cr/2$ snubber capacitors are free to charge and discharge —
          no main switch is actively short-circuiting the pole node.
\end{itemize}
The instant any one of those conditions changes (e.g.\ $D_1$ clamps, or
$A_1$ turns off) the circuit topology changes and different equations take
over.

\medskip
\textbf{The two circuit equations for this condition.}

\textit{KCL at the pole node:}
$\ir$ flows in; $\IL$ flows out to the load; the rest charges the combined
snubber capacitance $\Cr$ (= $\Cr/2$ in parallel with $\Cr/2$, seen from
the pole node):
\begin{equation}
    \frac{d\vf}{dt} = \frac{\ir - \IL}{\Cr}
    \label{eq:dvf_dt}
\end{equation}

\textit{KVL around the auxiliary loop ($A_1$--$\Lr$--pole\,node):}
The DC midpoint sits at $\Vdc/2$.
The pole node sits at $\vf$.
The full voltage difference $(\Vdc/2 - \vf)$ appears across $\Lr$ and
drives the rate of change of $\ir$:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{d\ir}{dt} = \frac{\Vdc/2 - \vf}{\Lr}
    \label{eq:dir_dt}
\end{equation}

\medskip
\textbf{Step 1 — Combine into one equation for $\vf$ alone.}

Equation~\eqref{eq:dvf_dt} involves both $\vf$ and $\ir$.
Equation~\eqref{eq:dir_dt} tells us how $\ir$ changes.
To get a single equation in $\vf$ only, differentiate
\eqref{eq:dvf_dt} once with respect to time:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d^2\vf}{dt^2} = \frac{1}{\Cr}\,\frac{d\ir}{dt}
\end{equation*}
The right-hand side contains $d\ir/dt$, which is exactly what
\eqref{eq:dir_dt} gives us.  Substitute:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d^2\vf}{dt^2}
        = \frac{1}{\Cr}\cdot\frac{\Vdc/2 - \vf}{\Lr}
        = \frac{\Vdc/2}{\Lr\Cr} - \frac{\vf}{\Lr\Cr}
\end{equation*}
Move the $\vf$ term to the left.  Define $\omegar \triangleq 1/\sqrt{\Lr\Cr}$
so that $\omegar^2 = 1/(\Lr\Cr)$:
\begin{equation}
    \ddot{\vf} + \omegar^2\,\vf = \omegar^2\cdot\frac{\Vdc}{2}
    \label{eq:oscillator}
\end{equation}
This is a \emph{forced harmonic oscillator} — the same form as a
spring-mass system driven by a constant force.  The right-hand side acts
as the ``equilibrium point'': $\vf$ wants to settle at $\Vdc/2$
(the DC midpoint), not at zero.

\medskip
\textbf{Step 2 — Simplify by measuring from the equilibrium point.}

The equilibrium of \eqref{eq:oscillator} sits at $\vf = \Vdc/2$.
Working directly with $\vf$ keeps dragging this constant around.
A cleaner approach is to measure $\vf$ \emph{relative to} the equilibrium.
Define:
\begin{equation*}
    u \triangleq \vf - \frac{\Vdc}{2}
    \qquad (\text{deviation of pole voltage from DC midpoint})
\end{equation*}
Also, notice that $\IL$ is a constant (load current changes slowly compared
to the commutation), so define:
\begin{equation*}
    j \triangleq \ir - \IL
    \qquad (\text{auxiliary current above the load current})
\end{equation*}
Since $\IL$ is constant, $\dot{j} = \dot{\ir}$.
Now rewrite \eqref{eq:dvf_dt} and \eqref{eq:dir_dt} in terms of $u$ and $j$:
\begin{align*}
    \dot{u} = \dot{\vf}
        &= \frac{\ir - \IL}{\Cr} = \frac{j}{\Cr} \\[6pt]
    \dot{j} = \dot{\ir}
        &= \frac{\Vdc/2 - \vf}{\Lr}
         = \frac{\Vdc/2 - (u + \Vdc/2)}{\Lr}
         = \frac{-u}{\Lr}
\end{align*}
The constant $\Vdc/2$ has dropped out completely.
Defining $\Zr \triangleq \sqrt{\Lr/\Cr}$ (the characteristic impedance of
the LC circuit), the final system is:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{u} = \frac{j}{\Cr}, \qquad \dot{j} = -\frac{u}{\Lr}
    \label{eq:shifted_states}
\end{equation}
These two equations are symmetric and coupled: $u$ drives $j$, and
$j$ drives $u$ back. This back-and-forth is the hallmark of oscillation.

\medskip
\textbf{Step 3 — What shape does the trajectory trace?}

From Step~1 we already know that $u$ satisfies
$\ddot{u} + \omegar^2 u = 0$ — simple harmonic motion (SHM), the same
equation as a mass on a spring with no friction.
Every student of SHM knows that the general solution is a sinusoid:
\begin{equation*}
    u(t) = u_0 \cos(\omegar t) + C\,\sin(\omegar t)
\end{equation*}
where $u_0 = u(0)$ is fixed by the initial condition and $C$ is determined
by the initial velocity.
From \eqref{eq:shifted_states}, $\dot{u} = j/\Cr$, so at $t=0$:
\begin{equation*}
    \dot{u}(0) = \frac{j_0}{\Cr}
    \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
    C\,\omegar = \frac{j_0}{\Cr}
    \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
    C = \frac{j_0}{\Cr\,\omegar}
\end{equation*}
Now $\Cr\,\omegar = \Cr/\sqrt{\Lr\Cr} = \sqrt{\Cr/\Lr} = 1/\Zr$,
so $C = j_0\Zr$.  The explicit solution for $u(t)$ is:
\begin{equation}
    u(t) = u_0\cos(\omegar t) + j_0\Zr\,\sin(\omegar t)
    \label{eq:u_solution}
\end{equation}

Now get $j(t)$ directly from \eqref{eq:shifted_states}: $j = \Cr\dot{u}$.
Differentiate \eqref{eq:u_solution} and multiply by $\Cr$:
\begin{align*}
    j(t) &= \Cr\bigl[-u_0\,\omegar\sin(\omegar t)
                      + j_0\Zr\,\omegar\cos(\omegar t)\bigr] \\[4pt]
         &= \Cr\,\omegar\bigl[-u_0\sin(\omegar t)
                               + j_0\Zr\cos(\omegar t)\bigr]
\end{align*}
Using $\Cr\,\omegar = 1/\Zr$ again:
\begin{equation}
    j(t) = -\frac{u_0}{\Zr}\sin(\omegar t) + j_0\cos(\omegar t)
    \label{eq:j_solution}
\end{equation}

\medskip
We now have explicit expressions for both $u(t)$ and $j(t)$.
Define two new coordinates:
\begin{equation*}
    X \triangleq u, \qquad Y \triangleq j\,\Zr
\end{equation*}
($Y$ is simply $j$ rescaled so that both axes carry the same units of
volts.)  Writing out $X$ and $Y$:
\begin{align*}
    X &= u_0\cos(\omegar t) + j_0\Zr\,\sin(\omegar t) \\[4pt]
    Y &= j\Zr = -u_0\sin(\omegar t) + j_0\Zr\,\cos(\omegar t)
\end{align*}
Now compute $X^2 + Y^2$, expanding term by term:
\begin{align*}
    X^2 &= u_0^2\cos^2(\omegar t)
           + 2u_0 j_0\Zr\cos(\omegar t)\sin(\omegar t)
           + j_0^2\Zr^2\sin^2(\omegar t) \\[4pt]
    Y^2 &= u_0^2\sin^2(\omegar t)
           - 2u_0 j_0\Zr\sin(\omegar t)\cos(\omegar t)
           + j_0^2\Zr^2\cos^2(\omegar t)
\end{align*}
Adding: the cross terms ($\pm 2u_0 j_0\Zr\cos\sin$) cancel exactly, and
$\cos^2 + \sin^2 = 1$ collapses each remaining pair:
\begin{equation}
    X^2 + Y^2
        = u_0^2\underbrace{(\cos^2 + \sin^2)}_{1}
        + j_0^2\Zr^2\underbrace{(\sin^2 + \cos^2)}_{1}
        = u_0^2 + (j_0\Zr)^2
    \label{eq:circle}
\end{equation}
The right-hand side is a \emph{constant} (set by the initial conditions,
not by time).  Therefore, as $t$ increases, the point $(X, Y) = (u,\,j\Zr)$
moves along a \textbf{circle} of radius:
\begin{equation*}
    R = \sqrt{u_0^2 + (j_0\Zr)^2}
\end{equation*}
centred at the origin of the $(u,\,j\Zr)$ plane, which is the point
$(\vf,\,j\Zr) = (\Vdc/2,\,0)$ in the original coordinates.

\medskip
\textbf{Initial conditions and the radius of the circle.}

Just before the resonant swing begins, $D_2$ is still conducting and
pins $\vf = 0$.  The boost phase has already ramped $\ir$ up to
$\IL + i_\text{boost}$.  So:
\begin{equation}
    u_0 = 0 - \frac{\Vdc}{2} = -\frac{\Vdc}{2},
    \qquad
    j_0 = (\IL + i_\text{boost}) - \IL = i_\text{boost}
    \label{eq:swing_ic}
\end{equation}
Substituting into \eqref{eq:circle}:
\begin{equation}
    R = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\Vdc}{2}\right)^{\!2} + (i_\text{boost}\,\Zr)^2}
    \label{eq:radius}
\end{equation}
\textbf{How does the phase plane connect to time?}

The phase plane has no time axis, so it is not obvious how a longer or
shorter arc corresponds to a longer or shorter commutation.
The answer comes directly from the parametric solution
\eqref{eq:u_solution}--\eqref{eq:j_solution}, which we can write as:
\begin{equation*}
    X(t) = u(t), \qquad Y(t) = j(t)\Zr,
    \qquad \text{where the parameter is } \theta = \omegar t
\end{equation*}
Every time step $\Delta t$ advances the angle $\theta$ by exactly
$\omegar\,\Delta t$, regardless of where on the circle the trajectory
currently sits.
Therefore the time taken to travel from the start angle $\theta_\text{start}$
to the end angle $\theta_\text{end}$ is:
\begin{equation*}
    T_\text{comm} = \frac{\theta_\text{end} - \theta_\text{start}}{\omegar}
\end{equation*}
A smaller swept angle means a shorter commutation time.

\textbf{How does $i_\text{boost}$ change the swept angle?}

All three trajectories in \figref{fig:phase_plane_3ICs} start at the
same $\vf = 0$ (i.e.\ $u_0 = -\Vdc/2$) but at different heights
$j_0\Zr = i_\text{boost}\Zr$ on the vertical axis.
The target is always $\vf = \Vdc$ (i.e.\ $u = +\Vdc/2$).
A higher starting point is already closer (in angle) to the target on the
right side of the circle, so less angle needs to be swept.
The extreme case is $i_\text{boost} = 0$: the trajectory starts exactly on
the horizontal axis at $(-\Vdc/2,\;0)$ and must sweep a full
$\pi$\,radians (a semicircle) to reach $(+\Vdc/2,\;0)$, taking time
$\pi/\omegar$.
Every additional ampere of boost lifts the starting point, reduces the
angle to be swept, and shortens the commutation time.
\end{eqnbox}

\begin{physbox}
% ── 1. Why ZVS eliminates switching loss ─────────────────────────────────────
\textbf{1.\ Why ZVS eliminates switching loss}

Consider closing any switch (here an IGBT $S_1$) in a circuit.
The switch transitions from fully off to fully on in a short but finite time
$t_\text{sw}$.  During that transition:
\begin{itemize}
    \item there is a voltage $V_{S_1}$ across the switch (it has not yet
          fully turned on), \emph{and}
    \item there is a current $I_{S_1}$ flowing through it (the load is
          already demanding current).
\end{itemize}
Instantaneous power dissipated in the switch $= V_{S_1} \times I_{S_1}$.
Integrated over the transition, this gives an \textbf{energy loss every
single switching cycle}.  At high switching frequencies (tens of kHz to MHz)
these losses add up and heat the switch, reducing efficiency and requiring
large heatsinks.

\textbf{Zero-voltage switching} eliminates this loss by a simple idea:
\emph{ensure the voltage across $S_1$ is zero at the exact moment you
fire it.}  Then $P = 0 \times I_{S_1} = 0$ regardless of how large the
current is — the switch turns on for free.

\medskip
\textbf{How does the ARCP achieve ZVS for $S_1$?}

The voltage across $S_1$ is
\begin{equation*}
    V_{S_1} = \Vdc - \vf
\end{equation*}
because the upper terminal of $S_1$ sits at $\Vdc$ and the lower terminal
(the pole node) sits at $\vf$.  For $V_{S_1} = 0$ we need
$\vf = \Vdc$.

So the entire job of the resonant swing is:
\emph{drive $\vf$ from 0 all the way up to $\Vdc$ before $S_1$ is fired.}
Once $\vf$ reaches $\Vdc$, the diode $D_1$ clamps it there — $D_1$ is the
anti-parallel diode of $S_1$, with its cathode at the positive rail ($\Vdc$)
and its anode at the pole node ($\vf$).
The moment $\vf$ tries to \emph{exceed} $\Vdc$, the pole node (anode of
$D_1$) rises fractionally above the positive DC rail (cathode of $D_1$);
$D_1$ becomes forward-biased and starts conducting, diverting $\ir$ away
from \emph{both} $\Cr/2$ snubber capacitors and into the positive DC rail
directly.
To see why both are involved: during the resonant swing $\ir$ was
simultaneously charging the $\Cr/2$ across $S_2$ (from 0 up to $\Vdc$)
\emph{and} discharging the $\Cr/2$ across $S_1$ (from $\Vdc$ down to 0).
When $\vf = \Vdc$, both processes complete at the same instant —
the $\Cr/2$ across $S_2$ is fully charged, and the $\Cr/2$ across $S_1$
is fully discharged to zero.
$D_1$ conducts and locks $\vf$ at $\Vdc$, so neither cap can change
further; $\ir$ flows through $D_1$ into the bus instead.
$S_1$ now has $V_{S_1} = \Vdc - \vf = 0$ and can be gated on with no
switching loss.

\medskip
% ── 2. Why R >= Vdc/2 is the ZVS geometric condition ─────────────────────────
\textbf{2.\ Why $R \geq \Vdc/2$ is the ZVS geometric condition}

We proved that the resonant swing traces a circle in the
$(\vf,\,j\Zr)$ plane with:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Centre} at $\vf = \Vdc/2$ (the DC midpoint)
    \item \textbf{Radius} $R = \sqrt{({\Vdc}/{2})^2 + (j_0\Zr)^2}$
\end{itemize}
The rightmost point the trajectory can reach is
\begin{equation*}
    \vf^\text{max} = \frac{\Vdc}{2} + R
\end{equation*}
For $\vf$ to reach $\Vdc$ (the ZVS target), we need:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\Vdc}{2} + R \geq \Vdc
    \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
    R \geq \frac{\Vdc}{2}
\end{equation*}
Substituting the radius formula:
\begin{equation*}
    \sqrt{\left(\frac{\Vdc}{2}\right)^{\!2} + (j_0\Zr)^2} \geq \frac{\Vdc}{2}
\end{equation*}
This is automatically true for \emph{any} $j_0 \geq 0$, because
$\sqrt{a^2 + b^2} \geq a$ whenever $b \geq 0$.

\medskip
\textbf{What goes wrong when $j_0 < 0$?}

$j_0 = \ir(0) - \IL$ is the auxiliary current \emph{above} the load current
at the start of the swing.  If $j_0 < 0$ (i.e.\ $\ir < \IL$), the net current
charging $\Cr$ is negative: from equation~\eqref{eq:dvf_dt},
$\dot{\vf} = j_0/\Cr < 0$.
The pole voltage \emph{falls} initially instead of rising.
In the phase plane this means the trajectory starts going in the wrong
direction — downward and to the left — before eventually looping back.
Physically, diode $D_2$ immediately clamps $\vf$ at 0 and prevents
it from going negative, so the commutation \emph{fails}.
This is visible in the IC-1 trajectory ($j_0=0$) of
Figure~\ref{fig:phase_plane_3ICs}: the trajectory starts horizontally
(neither rising nor falling), which is the marginal case.

\medskip
% ── 3. Why a boost phase is needed ───────────────────────────────────────────
\textbf{3.\ Why a boost phase is needed}

At light load the current $\IL$ is small.
When $S_2$ is turned off, the only current available to charge $\Cr$ and
raise $\vf$ is $\IL$ itself ($\ir = 0$ initially).  If $\IL$ is too small,
$\vf$ rises too slowly and cannot reach $\Vdc$ within the available
commutation window — $S_1$ would have to be fired before $\vf = \Vdc$,
causing hard switching and energy loss.

The boost phase solves this by pre-charging the resonant inductor $\Lr$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $A_1$ fires briefly; $\ir$ ramps up linearly from 0 to
          $\IL + i_\text{boost}$.
    \item $A_1$ turns off; the swing begins with
          $j_0 = i_\text{boost} > 0$.
    \item A positive $j_0$ gives a radius $R > \Vdc/2$, the trajectory
          goes in the right direction, and $\vf$ reaches $\Vdc$ — ZVS
          is achieved even at light load.
\end{enumerate}
The larger $i_\text{boost}$ is, the larger $R$ becomes, and the more
quickly $\vf$ swings to $\Vdc$.  At heavy load ($\IL$ large), the load
current alone is sufficient ($j_0 = 0$ is the marginal case) and no
boost is needed — this is called \emph{natural commutation}.

\medskip
% ── 4. Cr design trade-off: commutation energy vs turn-off stress ────────────
\textbf{4.\ $\Cr$ design trade-off: commutation energy vs.\ turn-off $dv/dt$ stress}

\textit{Reason to make $\Cr$ small — reduce commutation energy:}
Each time the pole commutates, $\Cr$ must be fully charged from 0 to
$\Vdc$ (or discharged from $\Vdc$ to 0).
The energy moved in and out of $\Cr$ every commutation is
\begin{equation*}
    E_\Cr = \tfrac{1}{2}\Cr\Vdc^2
\end{equation*}
This energy is supplied by the auxiliary inductor $\Lr$ (via the boost
and resonant swing) and ultimately comes from the DC bus.
Smaller $\Cr$ means less energy wasted on every switching event.

\textit{Reason not to make $\Cr$ too small — limit the voltage slew rate:}
When $S_2$ turns off, its current does not disappear instantly — it
diverts into $\Cr$ and begins charging it.
The rate at which the voltage across $S_2$ rises is
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d\vf}{dt} = \frac{\ir - \IL}{\Cr}
\end{equation*}
If $\Cr$ is very small, this rate becomes very large — the voltage
across $S_2$ spikes up extremely fast.
Real IGBTs and MOSFETs have a finite tolerance for how fast their
blocking voltage can rise ($dv/dt$ rating).
Exceeding it causes extra losses, gate-ringing, or device failure.

\textit{Summary — the design trade-off:}
Choose $\Cr$ small enough that $E_\Cr = \frac{1}{2}\Cr\Vdc^2$ is
acceptably small, yet large enough that the turn-off $dv/dt$ stays
within the switch's rating.
\end{physbox}

\section{Commutation from a Conducting Diode — P1 Fig.\,7}
\label{sec:P1Fig7}

\subsection*{Big picture — what is Fig.\,7 teaching?}

An inverter pole-leg has one job: connect the output node (the pole node,
$\vf$) to either the positive rail ($\Vdc$) or the negative rail (0\,V).
Switching between these two states is called \textbf{commutation}.

In a hard-switched inverter you would simply turn off the lower device and
fire the upper device.  That sounds simple, but during the brief transition
the switch carries both a large voltage \emph{and} a large current
simultaneously, dissipating energy as heat on every single switching cycle.

Fig.\,7 answers the question: \emph{how does the ARCP solve this, step by
step, starting from the moment $D_2$ is conducting?}

The word ``from diode'' describes the \emph{starting condition}: the load
current $\IL$ is flowing upward through the pole node into the load, and the
only path available for this current on the lower side is the anti-parallel
diode $D_2$.  The converter must now move $\vf$ from 0 all the way up to
$\Vdc$ so that the upper switch $S_1$ can be turned on at zero voltage
(ZVS).  Fig.\,7 shows the exact sequence of seven circuit states ---
(a)\,through\,(g) --- that accomplish this safely.

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{How each state is drawn.}
Every sub-figure in Fig.\,7 is a pair of two panels:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Top panel --- the waveform strip.}
          This shows $\ir(t)$ and $\vf(t)$ on a shared time axis covering
          the \emph{entire} commutation from state~(a) to state~(g).
          Vertical dashed lines divide the axis into strips; each strip is
          labelled at the bottom with the device(s) that are conducting
          during that interval (e.g.\ \textit{D2}, \textit{D2/A2},
          \textit{Cr/A2}, \textit{S1}).
          A horizontal reference line on the $\ir$ plot marks the load
          current level $\IL$.
          The sub-figure letter tells you which strip you are currently in.

    \item \textbf{Bottom panel --- the circuit snapshot.}
          This strips the full ARCP schematic down to only the devices

          circuit topology for the labelled time strip.
\end{itemize}

Reading both panels together for each state builds the complete physical
picture: you see \emph{where in time} you are (waveform) and
\emph{which path the current takes} (circuit snapshot) simultaneously.

\subsection*{State (a) — $D_2$ conducting: the resting state before commutation}

\figentry{fig:P1Fig7a}{P1_Fig7a_state1_D2_conducting}
    {P1 Fig.\,7(a) — $D_2$ conducting: initial resting state.
     $\vf = 0$, $\ir = 0$.
     Top panel: full commutation waveform with state~(a) strip highlighted.
     Bottom panel: circuit snapshot showing $D_2$ as the only active path.
     Source: De~Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,4.}{0.50}

\textbf{Top panel (waveform) — what you see in the state\,(a) strip.}

State~(a) occupies the leftmost strip of the waveform, labelled \textit{D2}.
Both traces are completely flat throughout this strip:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\ir = 0$\,A — no current flows in the resonant branch at all.
          The auxiliary inductor $\Lr$ is at rest.
    \item $\vf = 0$\,V — the pole node is pinned to the negative DC rail.
\end{itemize}
This strip has no fixed duration.  It lasts as long as the PWM lower-side
conduction period lasts — anywhere from a few microseconds to tens of
microseconds depending on the modulation index and load.
What matters is not how long state~(a) lasts, but the two numbers it
establishes before commutation begins:
\begin{equation}
    \vf(0^-) = 0\,\text{V}, \qquad \ir(0^-) = 0\,\text{A}
    \label{eq:stateA_ic}
\end{equation}
These are the initial conditions that every subsequent calculation in states
(b)\,through\,(g) is built on.

\medskip
\textbf{Bottom panel (circuit snapshot) — what is conducting and why.}

Only $D_2$ appears with a bold current path; all other devices are open.
The load current $\IL$ flows along the path:
\[
    \text{negative DC rail} \;\longrightarrow\; D_2 \;\longrightarrow\;
    \text{pole node} \;\longrightarrow\; L_F \;\longrightarrow\; \text{load}
\]
$D_2$ is not actively driven by any gate signal — it is a diode.
It conducts simply because $\IL$ pushes current in the upward direction
and $D_2$ is the only forward-biased path on the lower side of the pole.
The two snubber capacitors ($\Cr/2$ across $S_1$ and $\Cr/2$ across $S_2$)
sit at fixed voltages: the one across $S_1$ is charged to $\Vdc$
(full bus voltage); the one across $S_2$ is discharged to 0\,V
(short-circuited by the conducting $D_2$).
The main switches $S_1$, $S_2$ and both auxiliary back-to-back switches
$A_1$, $A_2$ are all open.

\medskip
\textbf{Why this state exists — what it does for the system.}

State~(a) is the \emph{resting state between switching events}.
Its role is to guarantee that when a commutation is eventually triggered,
the circuit starts from a fully known, clean condition
(equation~\eqref{eq:stateA_ic}).
If $\ir$ were not zero at the start — for example, if a previous
commutation had not fully reset the auxiliary branch — the resonant swing
would start from a different initial point and the ZVS condition might fail.
State~(a) ensures the slate is wiped clean every cycle.

% ── State (b) explanation ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection*{State (b) — $A_2$ fires: the boost phase begins}

\figentry{fig:P1Fig7b}{P1_Fig7b_state2_A1_boost_start}
    {P1 Fig.\,7(b) — $A_2$ fires: $\ir$ begins ramping linearly from zero.
     $D_2$ still conducts and keeps $\vf = 0$.
     Top panel: $\ir$ strip rising linearly in the \textit{D2/A2} region.
     Bottom panel: bold paths show $A_2$ and $D_2$ both conducting.
     Source: De~Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,4.}{0.50}

\textbf{What triggers this state.}

The control logic decides it is time to commutate from $D_2$ to $S_1$.
It fires the auxiliary switch $A_2$ --- the lower half of the back-to-back
bidirectional switch pair on the left of the pole leg
(see \figref{fig:P1Fig7b}, bottom panel).

\medskip
\textbf{Top panel (waveform) — what you see in the state\,(b) strip.}

We are now in the second strip, labelled \textit{D2/A2} in the waveform of
\figref{fig:P1Fig7a}.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\ir$ starts rising from 0 in a straight line --- a \emph{linear
          ramp}. The slope is constant throughout this strip.
    \item $\vf$ is still completely flat at 0\,V.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\textbf{Bottom panel (circuit snapshot) — two devices conducting at once.}

Two current paths are active simultaneously (both drawn bold):
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The new $A_2$--$\Lr$ path:}
          $A_2$ connects the DC bus midpoint (held at $\Vdc/2$ by the two
          $2C_{dc}$ capacitors) to one terminal of $\Lr$.
          The other terminal of $\Lr$ connects to the pole node, which is
          still clamped at 0\,V by $D_2$.
          So the full voltage $\Vdc/2 - 0 = \Vdc/2$ appears across $\Lr$,
          driving $\ir$ to ramp upward.

    \item \textbf{The continuing $D_2$--load path:}
          The load current $\IL$ still needs a return path to the negative
          rail.  $D_2$ provides it, carrying the \emph{difference}
          $\IL - \ir$ (which decreases as $\ir$ rises).
          While $D_2$ conducts, it clamps $\vf$ firmly at 0\,V.
          This in turn keeps the full $\Vdc/2$ across $\Lr$, maintaining
          the constant ramp rate.
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\textbf{Why the ramp is linear — the inductor equation.}

An inductor obeys $V_{\Lr} = \Lr\,\dfrac{d\ir}{dt}$.
Rearranging:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{d\ir}{dt} = \frac{V_{\Lr}}{\Lr} = \frac{\Vdc/2}{\Lr}
                    = \frac{\Vdc}{2\Lr}
    \label{eq:boost_ramp}
\end{equation}
The right-hand side is a constant (both $\Vdc$ and $\Lr$ are fixed).
A constant $d\ir/dt$ means $\ir$ increases at a \emph{constant rate} ---
a straight line on the waveform.
Starting from $\ir(0^-) = 0$:
\begin{equation}
    \ir(t) = \frac{\Vdc}{2\Lr}\,t
    \label{eq:ir_ramp}
\end{equation}

\medskip
\textbf{Why $\Vdc/2$ and not $\Vdc$?}

The auxiliary branch is connected to the \emph{DC bus midpoint}, not to
the full positive rail.  The midpoint is created by two $2C_{dc}$
capacitors that split the bus exactly in half.
Using the midpoint halves the driving voltage, which halves the ramp rate
and gives the control system more time to hit its current target precisely.
If the full $\Vdc$ were used, $\ir$ would ramp twice as fast and the timing
would be harder to control.

\medskip
\textbf{What ends this state.}

State~(b) ends when $\ir$ rises to exactly $\IL$.
At that point the current $D_2$ must supply becomes zero ($\IL - \ir = 0$),
and $D_2$ turns off \emph{naturally} --- no gate signal is needed, the
diode simply stops conducting because it has no more current to carry.
$A_2$ is \emph{not} turned off here; it stays on.
The control system detects (or predicts) this moment and immediately
fires $S_2$, which takes over from $D_2$ and keeps $\vf = 0$.
The circuit then enters state~(c).

% ── State (c) explanation ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection*{State (c) — boost complete: $\ir = \IL + i_\text{boost}$, resonant swing about to begin}

\figentry{fig:P1Fig7c}{P1_Fig7c_state3_boost_complete}
    {P1 Fig.\,7(c) — Boost complete.
     $\ir$ has reached $\IL + i_\text{boost}$; $\vf$ is still 0.
     $A_2$ and $S_2$ are both conducting; $D_2$ is off.
     Source: De~Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,4.}{0.50}

\textbf{Top panel (waveform) — what you see in the state\,(c) strip.}

We are now in the third strip of the waveform (\figref{fig:P1Fig7a}),
labelled \textit{S2/A2}.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\ir$ is \emph{still rising} linearly, continuing the ramp that
          started in state~(b), but now from $\IL$ toward
          $\IL + i_\text{boost}$.
          The slope is the same constant value $\Vdc/(2\Lr)$ as before,
          because $A_2$ is still on and $\vf$ is still held at 0\,V.
    \item $\vf$ remains flat at 0\,V --- clamped as before, just by a
          different device (see circuit snapshot below).
\end{itemize}
The strip ends when $\ir = \IL + i_\text{boost}$ and $S_2$ is turned off.

\medskip
\textbf{Bottom panel (circuit snapshot) — why did $D_2$ disappear and why is $S_2$ here?}

Two devices are bold in \figref{fig:P1Fig7c}: $A_2$ and $S_2$.
$D_2$ is no longer active.  Here is why each device changed role.

\medskip
\textbf{Why $D_2$ dropped out.}

$D_2$ is a diode.  A diode can only push current in one direction.
In state~(b), $D_2$ was supplying the \emph{deficit} current
$\IL - \ir > 0$ --- current flowing from the 0\,V rail into the pole node
to make up the difference between $\IL$ and the smaller $\ir$.
As $\ir$ climbed toward $\IL$, this deficit shrank.
The moment $\ir = \IL$, the deficit reached zero and $D_2$ had nothing
left to supply.  A diode carrying zero current simply turns off; there is
no gate signal involved --- it stops conducting on its own.

\medskip
\textbf{Why $S_2$ is fired.}

Once $D_2$ turns off, nothing is holding $\vf$ at 0\,V.
With $A_2$ still on and $\ir$ still flowing, the excess current
$\ir - \IL > 0$ would immediately start charging the snubber
capacitors and lifting $\vf$.
If we let that happen now, the resonant swing would begin with
$j_0 = \ir - \IL \approx 0$ --- barely above the marginal ZVS threshold.

To get a larger margin of safety --- a larger circle radius $R$ in the
phase plane --- we want $j_0 = i_\text{boost} > 0$ at the start of the swing.
The control achieves this by firing $S_2$ the instant $D_2$ turns off.

$S_2$ is an IGBT: an actively controlled switch that can carry current in
\emph{both} directions (unlike the diode $D_2$).
With $S_2$ on, the pole node is held at 0\,V by the switch, not the diode.
The excess current $\ir - \IL$ now flows \emph{down} through $S_2$ into
the negative rail (as shown by the downward arrow on $S_2$ in the circuit
snapshot).
This allows the boost to continue past $\IL$ while $\vf$ stays at 0\,V.

\medskip
\textbf{The full picture at the end of state (c).}

By the time $\ir$ reaches $\IL + i_\text{boost}$, we have:
\begin{equation}
    \vf = 0\,\text{V} \qquad \text{and} \qquad
    \ir = \IL + i_\text{boost}
    \label{eq:stateC_ic}
\end{equation}
These are the \emph{initial conditions for the resonant swing}.
In the shifted coordinates of the phase plane:
\begin{equation*}
    u_0 = \vf - \frac{\Vdc}{2} = -\frac{\Vdc}{2}, \qquad
    j_0 = \ir - \IL = i_\text{boost}
\end{equation*}
giving a circle radius $R = \sqrt{(\Vdc/2)^2 + (i_\text{boost}\,\Zr)^2}$,
which is larger the more boost current we add.

\medskip
\textbf{What ends this state — and how the resonant swing is triggered.}

\textit{Step 1 — What the control does.}

The control monitors $\ir$ and turns off $S_2$ the moment
$\ir = \IL + i_\text{boost}$ is reached.
$A_2$ stays on.

\medskip
\textit{Step 2 — Where was the current going before $S_2$ opened?}

Just before $S_2$ turns off, the current balance at the pole node is:
\begin{equation*}
    \underbrace{\ir}_{\text{in from } \Lr}
    \;=\;
    \underbrace{\IL}_{\text{out to load}}
    \;+\;
    \underbrace{(\ir - \IL)}_{\text{down through } S_2 \text{ to lower rail}}
\end{equation*}
$S_2$ is carrying the small excess $\ir - \IL = i_\text{boost}$ downward.
Everything is in balance and $\vf = 0$.

\medskip
\textit{Step 3 — What happens the instant $S_2$ opens?}

An open switch carries zero current.
The $i_\text{boost}$ that was flowing through $S_2$ \emph{must go somewhere}
--- current in an inductor cannot disappear instantaneously.
With $S_2$ open and $D_2$ already off, the only paths left at the pole node
are the load ($\IL$) and the two snubber capacitors.

\medskip
\textit{Why the effective capacitance at the pole node is the full $\Cr$,
not $\Cr/2$.}

When $S_2$ opens, the pole node is connected to two capacitors:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\Cr/2$ between the pole node and the \textbf{0\,V rail}
          (the snubber across $S_2$)
    \item $\Cr/2$ between the pole node and the \textbf{$\Vdc$ rail}
          (the snubber across $S_1$)
\end{itemize}
Both rails --- 0\,V and $\Vdc$ --- are held at \emph{fixed voltages} by
the large $2C_{dc}$ bus capacitors.
No matter what happens at the pole node, these rails do not move.
In circuit terms, both rails are \textbf{AC grounds}.

From the pole node's point of view, therefore, both $\Cr/2$ capacitors
are connected between the pole node and a fixed reference.
This makes them \textbf{effectively in parallel}:
\begin{equation*}
    C_\text{eff} = \frac{\Cr}{2} + \frac{\Cr}{2} = \Cr
\end{equation*}
When $\vf$ rises, the pole node must supply current to \emph{both}
capacitors simultaneously --- one charges toward $\Vdc$, the other
discharges toward 0\,V --- but both demand current from the same pole node.
The KCL at the pole node is therefore:
\begin{equation}
    \ir \;=\; \IL
        + \underbrace{\frac{\Cr}{2}\frac{d\vf}{dt}}_{\text{into }
              \Cr/2\text{ across }S_2}
        + \underbrace{\frac{\Cr}{2}\frac{d\vf}{dt}}_{\text{into }
              \Cr/2\text{ across }S_1}
    \;=\; \IL + \Cr\frac{d\vf}{dt}
    \label{eq:kcl_pole}
\end{equation}
Rearranging and evaluating at $t = 0^+$, where $\ir$ is still
$\IL + i_\text{boost}$ (inductor current cannot jump instantaneously):
\begin{equation}
    \Cr\,\frac{d\vf}{dt}\bigg|_{t=0^+}
        = \ir - \IL = i_\text{boost}
    \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
    \frac{d\vf}{dt}\bigg|_{t=0^+} = \frac{i_\text{boost}}{\Cr}
    \label{eq:vf_initial_slope}
\end{equation}
$\vf$ immediately starts rising from 0 at a rate set by $i_\text{boost}$
and the \emph{total} pole capacitance $\Cr$.
This is the first instant of the resonant LC swing --- state~(d) has begun.

\medskip
\textit{Step 4 — Why is the turn-off of $S_2$ relatively clean?}

In a hard-switched converter, a switch turns off while carrying the full
load current $\IL$ (which can be tens or hundreds of amperes).
The current snaps off abruptly and the energy $\frac{1}{2}L_\text{stray}I^2$
stored in stray wiring inductance causes a large voltage spike across the
switch.

Here $S_2$ carries only $i_\text{boost} = \ir - \IL$ at the moment it turns
off.
$i_\text{boost}$ is a \emph{control parameter} that the engineer can keep
small.
Moreover, the current does not snap off into stray inductance --- it
commutates smoothly into $\Cr$, which absorbs it without a spike.
$\vf$ rises at the finite rate $i_\text{boost}/\Cr$ rather than jumping
discontinuously.
This is why the turn-off of $S_2$ is described as
\emph{near-zero-current switching} (near-ZCS): the current being interrupted
is small and the voltage transition is smooth.

% ── State (d) explanation ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection*{State (d) — the resonant LC swing: $\vf$ rises from 0 to $\Vdc$}

\figentry{fig:P1Fig7d}{P1_Fig7d_state4_resonant_swing}
    {P1 Fig.\,7(d) — Resonant LC swing.
     $\vf$ rises in an S-curve from 0 to $\Vdc$; $\ir$ peaks then falls.
     $A_2$ remains on; both $\Cr/2$ snubbers are active (labelled).
     No main switch or diode conducts.
     Source: De~Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,4--5.}{0.50}

\textbf{State~(d) is the reason the ARCP exists.}

Everything in states (a)--(c) was preparation: ramping $\ir$ to the right
starting value, establishing the right initial voltage.
State~(d) is where those initial conditions are put to work: the resonant
swing carries $\vf$ all the way from 0 to $\Vdc$, enabling $S_1$ to be
turned on at zero voltage.

\medskip
\textbf{Top panel (waveform) — what you see in the state\,(d) strip.}

We are in the fourth strip of the waveform in \figref{fig:P1Fig7a},
labelled \textit{Cr/A2}.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\vf$ rises in a smooth \emph{S-shaped curve} from 0 towards
          $\Vdc$.  It does not ramp linearly --- it follows a sinusoidal
          shape, slow at first, fastest at the midpoint $\Vdc/2$, then
          slowing again as it approaches $\Vdc$.
    \item $\ir$ first \emph{rises} above its starting value
          $\IL + i_\text{boost}$, reaches a peak, then \emph{falls} back
          down.  The peak occurs when $\vf$ passes through $\Vdc/2$.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\textbf{Bottom panel (circuit snapshot) — only $A_2$, $\Lr$, and the two
$\Cr/2$ caps.}

Looking at \figref{fig:P1Fig7d}: $A_2$ is the only switch conducting.
Both $\Cr/2$ snubbers are explicitly labelled and shown with bold
connections --- one charging (across $S_1$, from 0 towards $\Vdc$) and one
discharging (across $S_2$, from $\Vdc$ towards 0).
All other devices --- $S_1$, $S_2$, $D_1$, $D_2$ --- are open.

\medskip
\textbf{Why $A_2$ must stay on during the swing.}

There are two reasons, one mandatory and one physical.

\textit{Reason 1 (mandatory) — an inductor carrying current cannot be
open-circuited.}

At the moment $S_2$ opens, $\Lr$ is carrying the current
$\ir = \IL + i_\text{boost}$.
An inductor obeys $V = \Lr\,d\ir/dt$.
If $A_2$ were turned off simultaneously with $S_2$, there would be no
closed path for $\ir$ to continue flowing --- the circuit through $\Lr$
would be broken.
The inductor cannot let $\ir$ drop to zero instantaneously, so it generates
a very large voltage spike across $A_2$ to try to maintain the current.
In practice this spike destroys the switch.
Turning off $A_2$ while $\ir > 0$ is therefore physically impermissible ---
$A_2$ \emph{must} remain on to keep the current path intact.

\textit{Reason 2 (physics) --- $A_2$ provides the forcing that centres the
oscillation at $\Vdc/2$.}

Once we accept that $A_2$ stays on (for the reason above), there is a
second, equally important consequence.
With $A_2$ on, the DC midpoint ($\Vdc/2$) is connected to $\Lr$, and the
KVL equation around the auxiliary loop contains the forcing term
$\Vdc/2 - \vf$.
This is exactly the forced harmonic oscillator derived in
Section~\ref{sec:P1Fig5}, whose equilibrium is at $\vf = \Vdc/2$.
The circular trajectory in the phase plane is centred at $\Vdc/2$, which
is what allows the swing to reach $\vf = \Vdc$ on the far side.
If $A_2$ were somehow removed while maintaining a current path (hypothetically),
the forcing term would vanish, the oscillation would centre around $\vf = 0$,
and $\vf$ would never reach $\Vdc$.

With $A_2$ on, the DC midpoint ($\Vdc/2$) remains connected to $\Lr$
throughout the swing.
This shifts the equilibrium of the oscillation to $\vf = \Vdc/2$ (the
forcing point), so the swing is centred at the midpoint of the bus and
can reach $\Vdc$ on the far side.
This is precisely the \emph{forced harmonic oscillator} we derived in the
green box of Section~\ref{sec:P1Fig5}.

\medskip
\textbf{The governing equations --- already derived in Section~\ref{sec:P1Fig5}.}

Check the three conditions from the green box against the current circuit:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $A_2$ is \textbf{on} --- DC midpoint connected to $\Lr$. \checkmark
    \item $D_2$ has \textbf{stopped conducting} --- it turned off at the
          end of state~(b). \checkmark
    \item Both $\Cr/2$ caps are \textbf{free} --- $S_2$ opened at the
          start of state~(d). \checkmark
\end{enumerate}
All three conditions are satisfied simultaneously for the first time.
The governing equations \eqref{eq:dvf_dt}--\eqref{eq:dir_dt} therefore
apply exactly here:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d\vf}{dt} = \frac{\ir - \IL}{\Cr}, \qquad
    \frac{d\ir}{dt} = \frac{\Vdc/2 - \vf}{\Lr}
\end{equation*}
Combining these (as shown in the green box) gives the forced harmonic
oscillator:
\begin{equation*}
    \ddot{\vf} + \omegar^2\,\vf = \omegar^2\,\frac{\Vdc}{2},
    \qquad \omegar = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\Lr\Cr}},\quad \Zr = \sqrt{\frac{\Lr}{\Cr}}
\end{equation*}

\medskip
\textbf{The solution --- a circular arc in the phase plane.}

As derived in the green box, the trajectory in the
$({\vf},\; j\Zr)$ phase plane is a \textbf{circle} centred at
$(\Vdc/2,\; 0)$ with radius:
\begin{equation*}
    R = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\Vdc}{2}\right)^{\!2} + (i_\text{boost}\,\Zr)^2}
\end{equation*}
The trajectory starts at $(\vf = 0,\; j\Zr = i_\text{boost}\Zr)$ and
travels counter-clockwise until it reaches $\vf = \Vdc$ on the right side
of the circle.
\figref{fig:phase_plane_3ICs} shows this arc for three values of
$i_\text{boost}$.

\medskip
\textbf{Why $\ir$ peaks at $\vf = \Vdc/2$.}

From the KCL equation $d\vf/dt = (\ir - \IL)/\Cr$:
$\vf$ is rising fastest when $\ir - \IL$ is largest, i.e.\ when $\ir$ is
at its peak.
In the phase plane, $\ir$ is maximum when the trajectory passes through
the topmost point of the circle, which occurs at $\vf = \Vdc/2$ (the
centre of the circle lies directly below this point).
After the peak, $\ir$ decreases while $\vf$ continues to rise towards
$\Vdc$.

\medskip
\textbf{What ends this state.}

State~(d) ends when $D_1$ clamps $\vf$ at $\Vdc$.
Here is the precise sequence.
The resonant arc carries enough energy to push $\vf$ \emph{above}
$\Vdc$: the phase-plane circle has radius
$R = \sqrt{(V_{dc}/2)^2+(i_\text{boost}Z_r)^2} > \Vdc/2$,
so its rightmost extent $\Vdc/2 + R$ lies above $\Vdc$ whenever
$i_\text{boost}>0$.
Throughout the swing $D_1$ is \emph{reverse}-biased — its anode (pole
node, $\vf$) is below its cathode (positive DC rail, $\Vdc$) — so it
stays off.
At $\vf = \Vdc$ the bias reaches exactly zero.
But $\ir$ still exceeds $\IL$ at this moment, so the circuit tries to
push $\vf$ higher still.
The instant the pole node rises fractionally above $\Vdc$, $D_1$ becomes
forward-biased, conducts, and shunts the excess current
$(\ir - \IL)$ into the positive DC bus.
The bus is held rigid at $\Vdc$ by the bulk DC capacitor — no matter how
much current flows in, the voltage barely moves.
$\vf$ is therefore pinned: $D_1$ clamps $\vf = \Vdc$, the resonant swing
stops, and the circuit enters state~(e).

% ── State (e) explanation ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection*{State (e) — $D_1$ clamps: ZVS window opens for $S_1$}

\figentry{fig:P1Fig7e}{P1_Fig7e_state5_upper_diode_clamp}
    {P1 Fig.\,7(e) — $D_1$ clamps $\vf = \Vdc$.
     $A_2$ still on; $D_1$ conducts the excess current $\ir - \IL$
     to the positive rail.
     $S_1$ sees zero volts and is ready to be fired at ZVS.
     Source: De~Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,5.}{0.50}

\textbf{Top panel (waveform) — what you see in the state\,(e) strip.}

We are now in the \textit{S1/A2} strip of the waveform in
\figref{fig:P1Fig7a}.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\vf$ is flat at $\Vdc$ --- clamped by $D_1$.
    \item $\ir$ is \emph{ramping downward} from its peak value
          $I_\text{peak}$ at a constant slope.
          The ramp is linear, mirroring the boost ramp of state~(b) but
          in the opposite direction.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\textbf{Bottom panel (circuit snapshot) — $D_1$ and $A_2$ both conducting.}

Two devices are bold in \figref{fig:P1Fig7e}: $D_1$ and $A_2$.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $A_2$ is still on --- for the same mandatory reason as in
          state~(d): $\Lr$ is carrying $\ir > 0$ and must not be
          open-circuited.
    \item $D_1$ is now conducting, but it did \emph{not} become
          forward-biased at the instant $\vf = \Vdc$.
          Throughout state~(d), $D_1$ was \emph{reverse}-biased
          (anode $= \vf <$ cathode $= \Vdc$).
          At $\vf = \Vdc$ the bias reached zero — $D_1$ was on the verge.
          Because $\ir > \IL$ at that moment, the circuit tried to push
          $\vf$ \emph{above} $\Vdc$, making the anode (pole node)
          fractionally higher than the cathode (positive rail) — that tiny
          excess instantly forward-biased $D_1$.
          $D_1$ then shunts the surplus current $(\ir - \IL)$ directly
          into the rigid $\Vdc$ bus, preventing $\vf$ from rising further.
          Result: $\vf$ is clamped at $\Vdc$.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\textbf{What $D_1$ clamping does to the current paths.}

Before $D_1$ clamped (state~d), the excess current $\ir - \IL$ was
flowing into the snubber capacitors, charging them and driving $\vf$
upward.
The moment $D_1$ conducts, it provides a \emph{lower-impedance} path
from the pole node directly to the positive DC rail.
The capacitors are now fully charged ($\Cr/2$ across $S_2$ is at $\Vdc$;
$\Cr/2$ across $S_1$ is at 0\,V) and cannot change further because
$D_1$ pins $\vf$.

The current balance at the pole node is now:
\begin{equation}
    \ir = \IL + i_{D_1}, \qquad
    i_{D_1} = \ir - \IL
    \label{eq:stateE_kcl}
\end{equation}
$D_1$ carries the excess $\ir - \IL$ to the positive rail.

\medskip
\textbf{Why $\ir$ ramps down linearly.}

With $A_2$ on and $\vf = \Vdc$ (held by $D_1$), the KVL around the
auxiliary loop gives:
\begin{equation}
    \Lr\,\frac{d\ir}{dt} = \frac{\Vdc}{2} - \vf
                         = \frac{\Vdc}{2} - \Vdc
                         = -\frac{\Vdc}{2}
    \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
    \frac{d\ir}{dt} = -\frac{\Vdc}{2\Lr}
    \label{eq:ir_rampdown}
\end{equation}
The right-hand side is a negative constant --- the same magnitude as the
boost ramp of state~(b) but with opposite sign.
$\ir$ therefore decreases at a \emph{constant rate}, tracing a straight
line downward on the waveform.

\medskip
\textbf{The ZVS window --- why $S_1$ can now be fired for free.}

The voltage across $S_1$ is $\Vdc - \vf$.
With $D_1$ clamping $\vf = \Vdc$:
\begin{equation*}
    V_{S_1} = \Vdc - \vf = \Vdc - \Vdc = 0\,\text{V}
\end{equation*}
$S_1$ sees \emph{exactly zero volts} across it.
Firing $S_1$ now costs zero switching energy:
$P_\text{sw} = V_{S_1} \times I_{S_1} = 0$.
This is the ZVS turn-on the entire commutation sequence was designed to
achieve.

The control must fire $S_1$ while $D_1$ is still conducting --- i.e.\
before $\ir$ ramps down to $\IL$.
If $S_1$ is fired too late (after $\ir < \IL$, so $i_{D_1} = 0$ and
$D_1$ turns off), $\vf$ would leave $\Vdc$ and the ZVS window would be
lost.

\medskip
\textbf{What ends this state.}

The control fires $S_1$ at zero voltage --- ZVS turn-on, lossless.
However, $D_1$ does \emph{not} turn off at this moment.
$\ir$ is still above $\IL$, so $D_1$ continues to carry the excess
$(\ir - \IL)$ to the positive DC rail, just as it has been doing since
the start of state~(e).
$A_2$ also remains \emph{gated on} --- it is still carrying $\ir$ from
the DC midpoint through $\Lr$ to the pole node.
The transition to state~(f) is not triggered by a control action but
by a circuit event: when $\ir$ falls to $\IL$, $D_1$'s current
$(\ir - \IL)$ reaches zero and $D_1$ turns off naturally.
At that instant the load current begins to transfer from the $D_1$ path
into $S_1$'s transistor, and the \emph{ramp-down} phase begins.

% ── State (f) explanation ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection*{State (f) — $\ir$ ramps to zero: auxiliary branch winds down}

\figentry{fig:P1Fig7f}{P1_Fig7f_state6_ir_ramps_down}
    {P1 Fig.\,7(f) --- Ramp-down phase: both $S_1$ and $A_2$ gated on;
     $\ir$ ramps from $\IL$ to zero as the load current transfers into $S_1$.
     Source: De~Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,5.}{0.50}

State~(f) --- called the \emph{ramp-down} phase by De~Doncker ---
begins the moment $\ir$ falls below $\IL$ and $D_1$ turns off naturally.
$S_1$ was already gated on (ZVS) during state~(e); now its transistor
takes over the load current as $\ir$ continues to decrease.
The only remaining task is to \emph{drain the magnetic energy in $\Lr$
to zero} while $A_2$ stays gated on and $S_1$ makes up the shortfall
in load current.

\medskip
\textbf{Top panel (waveform) — what you see in the state\,(f) strip.}

We are in the ramp-down strip of the waveform in \figref{fig:P1Fig7a}.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\vf$ remains flat at $\Vdc$ --- now held \emph{actively} by
          the closed switch $S_1$, not passively by the diode $D_1$.
    \item $\ir$ is falling in a \emph{straight line} toward zero.
          The descent is linear, the mirror image of the boost ramp in
          state~(b): same magnitude, opposite sign.
    \item The slope is exactly $-\Vdc/(2\Lr)$ --- derived below.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\textbf{Bottom panel (circuit snapshot) --- $S_1$ and $A_2$ both gated on.}

\figref{fig:P1Fig7f} shows both $S_1$ and $A_2$ in bold --- both
are \emph{actively gated on}.
$A_2$ is not relying on any body diode; its gate signal has not been
removed.
De~Doncker uses gate-controlled auxiliary devices (the paper mentions
MCTs, GTOs, or SCRs for $A_2$), and the gate is held asserted so that
$A_2$'s conducting transistor carries $\ir$ from the DC midpoint through
$\Lr$ into the pole node.

$S_1$ is also conducting through its transistor.
Unlike state~(e) --- where $\ir > \IL$ and the excess flowed through $D_1$
to the positive rail --- here $\ir < \IL$.
$S_1$'s transistor therefore carries the \emph{deficit} $(\IL - \ir)$
from the positive DC rail to the pole node, making up the shortfall
between what the load needs and what $\Lr$ provides.
KCL at the pole node:
\begin{equation*}
    \underbrace{\ir}_{\text{from }A_2/\Lr}
    +
    \underbrace{(\IL - \ir)}_{\text{from }S_1}
    = \IL \quad \text{(to load)}
\end{equation*}
As $\ir$ decreases toward zero, $S_1$ picks up more and more of $\IL$;
by the end of state~(f) $S_1$ carries the full load current.

\medskip
\textbf{Why the slope is $-\Vdc/(2\Lr)$ --- KVL argument.}

The auxiliary loop in state~(f) is topologically identical to state~(e):
$\Lr$ sits between the DC midpoint ($\Vdc/2$) and the pole node, with
$A_2$ (still gated on) providing the conduction path.
The only change is that $\vf = \Vdc$ is now enforced by a closed switch
$S_1$ rather than a diode $D_1$.
The KVL result is unchanged:
\begin{equation}
    \Lr\,\frac{d\ir}{dt}
        = \underbrace{\frac{\Vdc}{2}}_{\text{DC midpoint}}
        - \underbrace{\Vdc\vphantom{\frac{\Vdc}{2}}}_{\vf\ (\text{held by }S_1)}
        = -\frac{\Vdc}{2}
    \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
    \frac{d\ir}{dt} = -\frac{\Vdc}{2\Lr}
    \label{eq:ir_ramp_statef}
\end{equation}
The slope magnitude equals the boost ramp of state~(b).
The auxiliary inductor charges and discharges at the same rate:
the commutation is \emph{symmetric} about the resonant swing.

\medskip
\textbf{What ends this state.}

$\ir$ ramps to zero.
De~Doncker states explicitly in the paper:
\begin{quote}
\textit{``A2 will be gated off in anticipation of the current zero
in order to minimise reverse recovery losses.''}
\end{quote}
In other words, the controller removes $A_2$'s gate signal just
\emph{before} $\ir$ reaches zero rather than at the exact instant of
zero.
This ensures the residual current at $A_2$'s turn-off is negligible ---
a near-ZCS (zero-current switching) event: negligible current, negligible
reverse recovery charge, negligible switching loss.
$\Lr$ is now fully demagnetised; the auxiliary branch is electrically
invisible; $S_1$ carries the full $\IL$.
The circuit enters state~(g).

% ── State (g) explanation ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection*{State (g) — $S_1$ steady: commutation complete}

\figentry{fig:P1Fig7g}{P1_Fig7g_state7_S1_steady}
    {P1 Fig.\,7(g) --- Steady upper conduction: $S_1$ carries $\IL$,
     $\ir = 0$, auxiliary branch off.
     Source: De~Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,5.}{0.50}

State~(g) is the end of the commutation sequence.
The circuit has returned to a simple, quiet steady state --- but on the
\emph{opposite} rail from where it started.
State~(a) had $D_2$ clamping the pole node to the \emph{lower} rail
with $\ir = 0$.
State~(g) has $S_1$ holding the pole node to the \emph{upper} rail
with $\ir = 0$.
The ARCP has achieved what a hard-switched inverter would have done
with a brutal voltage-step turn-on of $S_1$ --- but entirely without
switching loss.

\medskip
\textbf{Top panel (waveform) — what you see in the state\,(g) strip.}

We are in the final strip of the waveform in \figref{fig:P1Fig7a}.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\vf$ is flat at $\Vdc$ --- unchanged from states (e) and (f).
    \item $\ir = 0$.
          The auxiliary inductor carries no current.
          Both waveforms are completely settled; there is nothing dynamic
          happening.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\textbf{Bottom panel (circuit snapshot) --- only $S_1$ conducting.}

Compare \figref{fig:P1Fig7g} with \figref{fig:P1Fig7a}.
They are mirror images of each other: state~(a) had only $D_2$ bold;
state~(g) has only $S_1$ bold.
Everything else --- $A_2$, $\Lr$, $D_1$, $D_2$, $S_2$ --- is off and
carries no current.
$A_2$ was gated off just before $\ir$ reached zero (state~f) and is now
fully open.
$\Lr$ is demagnetised: $\tfrac{1}{2}\Lr\ir^2 = 0$.
$S_1$ carries the full load current $\IL$ from the positive DC rail to
the pole node and on to the load.
The snubber capacitors are at their settled values:
$\Cr/2$ across $S_2$ is fully charged to $\Vdc$, and $\Cr/2$ across
$S_1$ is fully discharged to $0\,\text{V}$ --- exactly the starting
conditions for a future downward commutation ($S_1 \to D_2$).

\medskip
\textbf{What ends this state.}

Nothing ends it from within the circuit.
State~(g) persists until the \emph{outer} PWM control loop decides
it is time to switch the pole node back to the lower rail --- at which
point the downward commutation sequence (Fig.\,8 of De~Doncker's paper)
begins, using $A_1$ (the upper auxiliary switch) in the mirror-image
role that $A_2$ played here.

\begin{eqnbox}
\textbf{State (b) — linear boost ramp:}
\begin{equation}
    \ir(t) = \frac{\Vdc}{2\Lr}\,t, \qquad
    T_\text{boost} = \frac{2\Lr\,i_\text{boost}}{\Vdc}
    \label{eq:T_boost}
\end{equation}

\textbf{State (d) — resonant swing (forced LC oscillator):}
\begin{equation}
    \ddot{\vf} + \omegar^2\,\vf = \omegar^2\cdot\frac{\Vdc}{2},
    \qquad \omegar = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\Lr\Cr}},\quad \Zr = \sqrt{\frac{\Lr}{\Cr}}
    \label{eq:swing_ode}
\end{equation}

Solution with initial conditions $\vf(0)=0$, $\ir(0)=\IL+i_\text{boost}$:
\begin{align}
    \vf(t)  &= \frac{\Vdc}{2}(1 - \cos\omegar t)
               + (\ir(0)-\IL)\,\Zr\sin\omegar t
    \label{eq:vf_swing} \\[4pt]
    \ir(t)  &= \IL + (\ir(0)-\IL)\cos\omegar t
               + \frac{\Vdc/2}{\Zr}\sin\omegar t
    \label{eq:ir_swing}
\end{align}

\textbf{Peak current} (at end of state d, when $\vf = \Vdc$):
\begin{equation}
    I_\text{peak} = \IL + i_\text{boost} + \frac{\Vdc}{2\Zr}
    \label{eq:Ipeak_diode}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Resonant half-swing duration} (state d):
\begin{equation}
    T_\text{res} = \frac{\pi}{\omegar} = \pi\sqrt{\Lr\Cr}
    \label{eq:T_res}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Total commutation time} (states b through f):
\begin{equation}
    T_c = T_\text{boost} + T_\text{res}
        = \frac{2\Lr\,i_\text{boost}}{\Vdc} + \pi\sqrt{\Lr\Cr}
    \label{eq:T_comm_diode}
\end{equation}
\end{eqnbox}

\begin{physbox}
\textbf{Why seven states?} Each state has a distinct set of conducting devices,
so the governing equation changes at each transition.

\textbf{States (a)--(c)} are purely inductive: $\vf$ is pinned to 0 by $D_2$,
so the full $\Vdc/2$ appears across $\Lr$ and drives a linear current ramp.
This is energy storage, not switching.

\textbf{State (d)} is the only state where the LC oscillator is active.
The circular trajectory in the $(\vf,\, \ir-\IL)$ phase plane must reach
$\vf = \Vdc$ for ZVS.  Circle radius:
$R = \sqrt{i_\text{boost}^2 + (\Vdc/2\Zr)^2}$.
ZVS is satisfied when $i_\text{boost} \geq 0$.

\textbf{State (f)} recovers residual energy from $\Lr$ back into the DC bus
through $D_1$ — this is why the auxiliary circuit is lossless in the ideal case.

\textbf{State (g)} is the new idle state. The sequence (a)$\to$(g) is the
complete low-to-high ($D_2 \to S_1$) transition using $A_2$; the symmetric
high-to-low ($S_1 \to D_2$) transition uses $A_1$ instead and is covered
next in Fig.\,8.
\end{physbox}

% ============================================================

\subsection*{Big picture}

\figentry{fig:P1Fig8a}{P1_Fig8a_waveform_switch_low_current}
    {P1 Fig.\,8 --- Full commutation waveform for the downward transition
     ($S_1 \to D_2$, low load current).
     Top trace: auxiliary inductor current $\ir(t)$, which dips negative
     during the boost and resonant phases.
     Bottom trace: pole-node voltage $\vf(t)$, which falls smoothly from
     $\Vdc$ to $0$.
     Five dashed vertical lines divide the waveform into strips labelled
     \textit{S1}, \textit{S1/A1}, \textit{Cr/A1}, \textit{D2/A1},
     and \textit{D2}, corresponding to states~(b)--(f) in this document.
     Source: De~Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,5.}{0.75}

\figref{fig:P1Fig7a} (paper Fig.\,7) showed the ARCP pole leg commutating
\emph{upward} --- from the lower freewheeling diode $D_2$ (pole node at
$\vf = 0$) to the upper main switch $S_1$ (pole node at $\vf = \Vdc$),
using $A_2$ as the auxiliary switch.

\figref{fig:P1Fig8a} (paper Fig.\,8) is the \emph{mirror-image}
commutation: the pole leg switching \emph{downward} --- from the upper main
switch $S_1$ (conducting at $\vf = \Vdc$) to the lower freewheeling diode
$D_2$ (at $\vf = 0$), using $A_1$ (the upper auxiliary switch).

\medskip
\textbf{Step 1 — start with a capacitor.}

Before worrying about auxiliary switches and resonance, recall one
fundamental fact about capacitors:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{d\vf}{dt} = \frac{i_\text{pole}}{C_r}
    \label{eq:cap_discharge}
\end{equation}
The pole-node voltage $\vf$ can only change if current flows in or out of
the snubber capacitor $C_r$.
No current flowing $\Rightarrow$ $\vf$ stays frozen.
More current $\Rightarrow$ $\vf$ changes faster.
This single equation is the key to everything that follows.

\medskip
\textbf{Step 2 — what has to happen during a downward commutation?}

Before commutation: $S_1$ is on, $\vf = \Vdc$.
After commutation: $D_2$ (or $S_2$) must conduct, $\vf = 0$.
The snubber capacitor $C_r$ at the pole node is currently charged to
$\Vdc$.
For ZVS, $C_r$ must be \emph{fully discharged to $0$\,V before $S_2$ is
gated on}.
If $S_2$ is gated on while $\vf > 0$, the capacitor discharges
instantaneously through $S_2$ --- that is hard-switching, with all the
energy loss we are trying to avoid.

So the question reduces to: \emph{what discharges $C_r$?}

\medskip
\textbf{Step 3 — imagine $\Lr$ is absent: only $\IL$ discharges $C_r$.}

\textit{We temporarily remove the auxiliary circuit to understand the
problem first.  The full picture with $\Lr$ comes in Steps~5 and~6.}

Once $S_1$ is gated off it becomes an open circuit.
The load filter inductor $L_F$ forces its current $\IL$ to keep flowing
regardless --- that is what inductors do.
$\IL$ flows \emph{out of} the pole node toward the load:
\[
    \text{pole node} \;\xrightarrow{\;I_L\;}\; L_F \;\longrightarrow\;
    \text{load}
\]

\medskip
\textit{``But if current flows out of the pole node, can't $D_2$
immediately supply it from below?''}

No --- $D_2$ is reverse-biased the entire time $\vf > 0$.
Check the bias at the moment $S_1$ opens:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $D_2$ anode $= 0$\,V (negative DC rail --- fixed)
    \item $D_2$ cathode $= \vf = \Vdc$ (just released by $S_1$)
\end{itemize}
$D_2$ has the full bus voltage across it in the reverse direction.
It turns on only at the very end, when $\vf$ has fallen to (near) 0\,V
and forward-biases $D_2$.
Until then, the snubber capacitor $C_r$ is the only thing supplying $\IL$.
$C_r$ discharges, losing charge, and $\vf$ falls.

\medskip
\textit{``Then why does $\vf$ look like a sinusoid in the waveform?
Shouldn't it be a straight ramp?''}

Exactly right --- \textbf{without $\Lr$, the discharge would be a straight
ramp.}
With only $C_r$ and constant $\IL$, equation~\eqref{eq:cap_discharge}
gives:
\[
    \frac{d\vf}{dt} = -\frac{\IL}{C_r} = \text{constant}
    \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad
    \vf(t) = \Vdc - \frac{\IL}{C_r}\,t \quad\text{(straight line)}
\]
The sinusoidal shape you see in \figref{fig:P1Fig8a} is produced by
$\Lr$ --- we will come to that in Step~5.
For now, use the simpler straight-ramp model to derive the threshold.

\medskip
\textbf{Step 4 — how long does the straight-ramp discharge take?}

Starting from $\vf = \Vdc$ and falling at constant slope $\IL/C_r$, the
time to reach $\vf = 0$ is:
\begin{equation}
    T_\text{swing} = \frac{\Vdc\,C_r}{\IL}
    \label{eq:Tswing}
\end{equation}
The smaller $\IL$ is, the longer this takes.

\medskip
\textbf{Step 5 — the control has a time budget: this gives us $I_\text{th}$.}

The control cannot wait forever.
The switching period and deadtime set a maximum window $T_\text{max}$
within which $\vf$ must reach zero.
For ZVS we need $T_\text{swing} \leq T_\text{max}$, which gives:
\begin{equation}
    \IL \;\geq\; \underbrace{\frac{\Vdc\,C_r}{T_\text{max}}}_{\displaystyle I_\text{th}}
    \label{eq:Ith}
\end{equation}
$I_\text{th}$ is the \textbf{natural commutation threshold}.
It depends only on the circuit ($\Vdc$, $C_r$) and the control
($T_\text{max}$), not on the load.

\medskip
\textbf{Step 6 --- what goes wrong when $\IL < I_\text{th}$?}

When the load current is small:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\vf$ falls slowly --- equation~\eqref{eq:Tswing}.
    \item Before $\vf$ reaches zero, the control's deadtime expires.
    \item The control must gate $S_2$ on, even though $\vf > 0$.
    \item $S_2$ turns on with voltage across it --- hard switching.
          The energy $\tfrac{1}{2}C_r \vf^2$ dissipates instantly inside
          $S_2$.
\end{itemize}
This is exactly the loss the ARCP topology was designed to eliminate.

\medskip
\textbf{Step 7 --- the fix: bring $\Lr$ back into the picture.}

Now we restore the auxiliary circuit and see what $\Lr$ actually does.
$\Lr$ changes the problem in \emph{two} ways simultaneously.

\medskip
\textit{What $\Lr$ does (1): adds a second discharge current.}

Before $S_1$ is gated off, $A_1$ is fired.
With $S_1$ still \emph{on} and $\vf = \Vdc$, the full half-bus voltage
$\Vdc/2$ appears across $\Lr$ (pole node at $\Vdc$, DC midpoint at
$\Vdc/2$).
$\Lr$ is an inductor --- it responds by ramping up a current $\ir$
\emph{linearly}:
\[
    \frac{d\ir}{dt} = \frac{\Vdc}{2\Lr} \qquad
    \text{(during state~(b), S1 still on)}
\]
This is the \textbf{boost phase} --- $\Lr$ is pre-charged like a spring
being wound up, but $C_r$ does not discharge yet because $S_1$ is still
holding $\vf = \Vdc$.

When $S_1$ is then gated off, the pole node is freed and $\ir$ is already
flowing \emph{out} of it:
\[
    \text{pole node} \;\xrightarrow{\;\ir\;}\; \Lr \;\xrightarrow{}\;
    A_1 \;\longrightarrow\; \text{DC midpoint}
\]
Now both $\IL$ \emph{and} $\ir$ drain the pole node, and $C_r$
discharges faster than $\IL$ alone ever could.

\medskip
\textit{What $\Lr$ does (2): converts the straight ramp into a
sinusoid.}

Once $S_1$ opens, $\Lr$ and $C_r$ are in a \emph{coupled loop}.
As $C_r$ discharges and $\vf$ falls, the voltage across $\Lr$ changes,
which changes $\ir$, which changes how fast $C_r$ discharges, which
changes $\vf$ again --- a continuous feedback:

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{ccccc}
$\vf\!\downarrow$ & $\Rightarrow$ &
$\text{voltage across }\Lr\!\uparrow$ & $\Rightarrow$ &
$|\ir|\!\uparrow$ \\[2pt]
& & $\Uparrow$ & & $\Downarrow$ \\[2pt]
\multicolumn{3}{c}{$C_r$ discharges faster} & $\Leftarrow$ &
$\text{more current out of pole}$
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

This self-reinforcing loop is precisely an LC oscillator.
The governing ODE is:
\begin{equation}
    \ddot{\vf} + \omegar^2\,\vf = \omegar^2\,\frac{\Vdc}{2},
    \qquad \omegar = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\Lr C_r}}
    \label{eq:swing_ode_fig8}
\end{equation}
whose solution is a \textbf{sinusoid centred at $\Vdc/2$} --- exactly
the smooth S-curve seen in \figref{fig:P1Fig8a}.
Without $\Lr$, equation~\eqref{eq:cap_discharge} gives only a straight
ramp; $\Lr$ is what bends that ramp into the sinusoidal arc.

\medskip
\textit{The complete two-stage picture.}

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabular}{p{1.8cm} p{2.8cm} p{3.8cm} p{3.8cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{State} & \textbf{S1 status} & \textbf{What $\Lr$ is doing} &
\textbf{What $C_r$ is doing} \\
\midrule
(b) boost & \textbf{ON} --- holds $\vf=\Vdc$ &
  $\ir$ ramps linearly ($\dot{\ir}=\Vdc/2\Lr$) &
  Not discharging --- $\vf$ frozen \\
(c) swing  & \textbf{OFF} --- pole node freed &
  $\ir$ and $C_r$ locked in LC resonance &
  Discharging sinusoidally; $\vf: \Vdc \to 0$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\textbf{``Pre-loading $\Lr$ with boost energy''} means completing
stage~(b) \emph{before} $S_1$ is gated off, so that $\ir$ is already at
its target value the instant the LC resonance begins.
The initial value of $\ir$ sets the radius of the sinusoidal arc;
a larger $\ir$ at the start of stage~(c) guarantees a wide enough arc
to carry $\vf$ all the way from $\Vdc$ down to 0.

\medskip
\textbf{Three structural differences from \figref{fig:P1Fig7a} (paper Fig.\,7).}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Starting device is a switch, not a diode.}
          In \figref{fig:P1Fig7a}, $D_2$ was conducting passively --- no
          gate signal, just forward-biased by the load current, and it
          turned off \emph{naturally} when $\ir$ displaced its current.
          Here, $S_1$ is actively gated on and must be \emph{forced} off
          by the control at the right moment.

    \item \textbf{Direction reversed: $\vf$ falls from $\Vdc$ to $0$.}
          In \figref{fig:P1Fig7a}, $\vf$ rose from $0$ to $\Vdc$ and
          $D_1$ clamped the pole to the positive rail.
          Here, $\vf$ falls from $\Vdc$ to $0$ and $D_2$ clamps the pole
          to the negative rail.

    \item \textbf{Auxiliary current $\ir$ flows in the opposite direction.}
          In \figref{fig:P1Fig7a}, $\ir$ went positive: DC midpoint
          $\to$ $A_2$ $\to$ $\Lr$ $\to$ pole node, building energy to
          push $\vf$ upward.
          Here, $\ir$ goes negative: pole node $\to$ $\Lr$ $\to$ $A_1$
          $\to$ DC midpoint, building energy to pull $\vf$ downward.
          On the waveform of \figref{fig:P1Fig8a}, this appears as a
          downward dip in $\ir$ rather than an upward peak.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{What De~Doncker means by ``no ramp phases'' --- a shorter sequence.}

De~Doncker states that this sequence \textit{``is similar to that
described above except there are no ramp phases.''}
This statement needs careful reading --- $\ir$ \emph{does} still ramp
in state~(b) of this sequence (the \textit{S1/A1} boost strip).
What De~Doncker means is that the \emph{two-stage, device-handoff ramp
structure} of \figref{fig:P1Fig7a} disappears.

In \figref{fig:P1Fig7a} (upward commutation from diode $D_2$), two
separate pre-resonant ramp stages were forced on the circuit:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{State (b):} $\ir$ ramps from 0 to $\IL$ --- required
          to displace $D_2$'s current to zero so $D_2$ could turn off
          \emph{naturally}.  A diode has no gate; it cannot simply be
          commanded off.
    \item \textbf{State (c):} $S_2$ takes over from $D_2$; $\ir$
          ramps further from $\IL$ to $\IL + i_\text{boost}$ --- the
          additional boost needed above $\IL$.
\end{itemize}
Both stages existed purely because of the passive nature of $D_2$.

Here, the starting device $S_1$ is a \emph{gated switch} --- it can be
turned off at any instant the control chooses.
No device-handoff ramp is needed.
$\Lr$ is pre-charged during a single boost stage (state~(b), $S_1$
still on), and then $S_1$ is simply gated off.
The two-stage pre-resonant structure collapses into one stage, reducing
the total sequence from \textbf{seven states to five}:

The lower main switch $S_2$, the freewheeling diode $D_2$, the upper
freewheeling diode $D_1$, and both auxiliary back-to-back switches $A_1$,

\medskip
\textbf{Why this state exists --- what it does for the system.}

State~(a) is the \emph{resting state between switching events}, exactly as
state~(a) was for the upward commutation.
Its role is to guarantee that when the downward commutation is triggered, the
circuit starts from a fully known, clean condition
(equation~\eqref{eq:stateA8_ic}).
Specifically, $\ir = 0$ ensures the auxiliary inductor has no residual energy
left over from a previous event.
If $\ir$ were non-zero, the resonant swing would start from a different initial
point, potentially causing $D_2$ to clamp too early or too late --- both of
which break the ZVS condition for $S_2$.

\subsection*{State (b) --- $A_1$ fires: the boost phase begins}

\figentry{fig:P1Fig8c}{P1_Fig8c_state2_A1_boost_start}
    {P1 Fig.\,8(c) [our state~(b)] --- $A_1$ fires: $\ir$ begins ramping
     negatively from zero.
     $S_1$ is still gated on and keeps $\vf = \Vdc$.
     Top panel: $\ir$ dips downward linearly in the \textit{S1/A1} strip of
     \figref{fig:P1Fig8a}.
     Bottom panel: bold paths show $A_1$ and $S_1$ both conducting.
     Source: De~Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,5.}{0.50}

\textbf{What triggers this state.}

The control decides it is time to commutate the pole node from
$\vf = \Vdc$ down to $\vf = 0$.
It fires $A_1$ --- the upper half of the back-to-back bidirectional
auxiliary switch pair on the left of the pole leg
(see \figref{fig:P1Fig8c}, bottom panel).

\medskip
\textbf{Top panel (waveform) --- what you see in the state\,(b) strip.}

We are now in the second strip of the waveform in \figref{fig:P1Fig8a},
labelled \textit{S1/A1}.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\ir$ starts falling from 0 in a straight line --- a
          \emph{linear ramp in the negative direction}.
          The slope is constant throughout this strip.
    \item $\vf$ is still completely flat at $\Vdc$.
          $S_1$ is clamping the pole node to the positive rail, so
          $C_r$ does \emph{not} discharge during this state.
\end{itemize}
The downward dip in $\ir$ is the mirror image of the upward ramp seen
in state~(b) of \figref{fig:P1Fig7a} (paper Fig.\,7), where $A_2$
drove $\ir$ positive.

\medskip
\textbf{Bottom panel (circuit snapshot) --- two devices conducting at once.}

Two current paths are active simultaneously:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The new $A_1$--$\Lr$ path.}
          $A_1$ connects the DC bus midpoint (held at $\Vdc/2$ by the
          two large $2C_{dc}$ bus capacitors) to one terminal of $\Lr$.
          The other terminal of $\Lr$ connects to the pole node, which
          is held at $\Vdc$ by $S_1$.
          The voltage across $\Lr$ is therefore:
          \[
              V_{\Lr} = \Vdc - \frac{\Vdc}{2} = \frac{\Vdc}{2}
              \qquad\text{(pole end positive, midpoint end negative)}
          \]
          Current is driven from the pole node, through $\Lr$, through
          $A_1$, into the midpoint --- a \emph{downward} (negative)
          flow.

    \item \textbf{The continuing $S_1$--load path.}
          The load current $\IL$ still flows from the positive rail
          through $S_1$ to the pole node and on to the load.
          But now the auxiliary branch is also drawing current
          $|\ir|$ out of the pole node.
          $S_1$ must supply both:
          \[
              I_{S_1} = \IL + |\ir|
          \]
          As $|\ir|$ grows, $S_1$ carries progressively more current.
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\textbf{Why the ramp is linear --- the inductor equation.}

An inductor obeys $V_{\Lr} = \Lr\,\dfrac{d\ir}{dt}$.
Because $V_{\Lr} = \Vdc/2$ and is \emph{constant} throughout this
state (both $S_1$ and the bus midpoint hold their ends of $\Lr$ at
fixed voltages), the ramp rate is constant:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{d\ir}{dt} = -\frac{\Vdc}{2\Lr}
    \label{eq:boost_ramp_fig8}
\end{equation}
The negative sign reflects the downward direction.
Starting from $\ir(0^-) = 0$:
\begin{equation}
    \ir(t) = -\frac{\Vdc}{2\Lr}\,t
    \label{eq:ir_ramp_fig8}
\end{equation}
This is a straight line falling at a constant slope --- the linear dip
visible in the \textit{S1/A1} strip of \figref{fig:P1Fig8a}.

\medskip
\textbf{Why $\Vdc/2$ and not $\Vdc$?}

The auxiliary branch connects to the \emph{DC bus midpoint}, not to the
negative rail.
Using the midpoint halves the driving voltage and therefore halves the
ramp rate, giving the control more time to hit its current target
precisely.
This is identical reasoning to state~(b) of \figref{fig:P1Fig7a}
(paper Fig.\,7).

\medskip
\textbf{Why $S_1$ must remain on during this state.}

This is the critical design choice of the low-current commutation.
As shown in Step~7 of the Big~Picture, state~(b) is the
\emph{pre-charge stage}: $\Lr$ is wound up with energy while $C_r$ is
held frozen at $\Vdc$ by $S_1$.
If $S_1$ were opened now, the LC resonance would begin immediately ---
but with $|\ir|$ still too small, the resonant arc would not be wide
enough to carry $\vf$ all the way from $\Vdc$ down to 0.
$D_2$ would never clamp and ZVS of $S_2$ would fail.
$S_1$ stays on \emph{exactly long enough} to charge $\Lr$ to the
required current target before the swing begins.

\medskip
\textbf{What ends this state.}

State~(b) ends when the control determines that $|\ir|$ has reached the
target boost level --- enough stored energy in $\Lr$ to guarantee that
the resonant swing in state~(c) will carry $\vf$ all the way to 0.
At that moment the control gates \emph{$S_1$ off}.
$A_1$ is \emph{not} turned off here; it stays on.
With $S_1$ open, the pole node is freed and the LC resonance begins ---
state~(c).

\subsection*{State (c) --- the resonant LC swing: $\vf$ falls from $\Vdc$ to 0}

\figentry{fig:P1Fig8d}{P1_Fig8d_state3_resonant_swing}
    {P1 Fig.\,8(d) [our state~(c)] --- Resonant LC swing.
     $\vf$ falls in an S-curve from $\Vdc$ to 0; $|\ir|$ peaks then
     decreases.
     $A_1$ remains on; both $\Cr/2$ snubbers are active.
     No main switch or diode conducts.
     Source: De~Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,5.}{0.50}

\textbf{State~(c) is the core of the downward commutation.}

Everything in states~(a) and~(b) was preparation: establishing the
resting condition, then charging $\Lr$ to the correct boost current.
State~(c) is where that stored energy is put to work: the resonant
swing carries $\vf$ from $\Vdc$ all the way down to 0, enabling $S_2$
to be turned on at zero voltage.
It is the mirror image of state~(d) in \figref{fig:P1Fig7a}
(paper Fig.\,7), which swung $\vf$ upward from 0 to $\Vdc$.

\medskip
\textbf{Top panel (waveform) --- what you see in the state\,(c) strip.}

We are in the third strip of the waveform in \figref{fig:P1Fig8a},
labelled \textit{Cr/A1}.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\vf$ falls in a smooth \emph{S-shaped curve} from $\Vdc$
          towards 0.
          It does not ramp linearly --- it follows a sinusoidal shape,
          slow at first, fastest at the midpoint $\Vdc/2$, then
          slowing again as it approaches 0.
    \item $\ir$ is already negative at the start (from the boost in
          state~(b)).
          Its magnitude first \emph{increases} (dips deeper) until
          $\vf$ passes through $\Vdc/2$, then \emph{decreases} (dip
          becomes shallower) as $\vf$ approaches 0.
          The deepest point of the dip --- labelled $I_\text{boost}$
          in \figref{fig:P1Fig8a} --- occurs at $\vf = \Vdc/2$.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\textbf{Bottom panel (circuit snapshot) --- only $A_1$, $\Lr$, and the
two $\Cr/2$ caps.}

Looking at \figref{fig:P1Fig8d}: $A_1$ is the only switch conducting.
Both $\Cr/2$ snubbers are active --- one discharging (across $S_1$,
from $\Vdc$ towards 0) and one charging (across $S_2$, from 0
towards $\Vdc$).
All other devices --- $S_1$, $S_2$, $D_1$, $D_2$ --- are open.
$D_2$ is still firmly reverse-biased: its anode is at 0\,V and its
cathode is at $\vf > 0$.

\medskip
\textbf{Why $A_1$ must stay on during the swing.}

The two reasons are identical to those for $A_2$ in state~(d) of
\figref{fig:P1Fig7a}, applied in mirror image.

\textit{Reason 1 (mandatory) --- an inductor carrying current cannot
be open-circuited.}

At the moment $S_1$ opens, $\Lr$ is already carrying the boost
current $\ir = -|\ir_0|$.
If $A_1$ were turned off simultaneously, the circuit through $\Lr$
would be broken.
$\Lr$ cannot let $\ir$ change instantaneously; it would generate a
destructive voltage spike.
$A_1$ must remain on to keep the current path intact.

\textit{Reason 2 (physics) --- $A_1$ provides the forcing that centres
the oscillation at $\Vdc/2$.}

With $A_1$ on, the DC midpoint ($\Vdc/2$) remains connected to $\Lr$
throughout the swing.
The KVL equation around the auxiliary loop contains the forcing term
$\Vdc/2 - \vf$, which centres the oscillation at $\vf = \Vdc/2$.
This is what allows the trajectory to swing from $\vf = \Vdc$ all the
way across to $\vf = 0$ on the other side.
Without the midpoint connection the oscillation would be centred
somewhere else and the swing would not reach the correct rail.

\medskip
\textbf{The governing equations --- same as state~(d) of
\figref{fig:P1Fig7a}.}

Check the three conditions for the LC oscillator (same as the
green box in Section~\ref{sec:P1Fig5}):
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $A_1$ is \textbf{on} --- DC midpoint connected to $\Lr$.
          \checkmark
    \item $S_1$ has \textbf{just opened} --- pole node is free.
          \checkmark
    \item Both $\Cr/2$ caps are \textbf{free} --- $S_1$ no longer
          clamps one end. \checkmark
\end{enumerate}
All three conditions are satisfied.
The governing equations are therefore identical to those of state~(d)
in \figref{fig:P1Fig7a}:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{d\vf}{dt} = \frac{\ir - \IL}{\Cr},
    \qquad
    \frac{d\ir}{dt} = \frac{\Vdc/2 - \vf}{\Lr}
    \label{eq:swing_odes_fig8}
\end{equation}
Combining these gives the forced harmonic oscillator
(equation~\eqref{eq:swing_ode_fig8} from the Big~Picture):
\begin{equation*}
    \ddot{\vf} + \omegar^2\,\vf = \omegar^2\,\frac{\Vdc}{2},
    \qquad \omegar = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\Lr\Cr}},
    \quad \Zr = \sqrt{\frac{\Lr}{\Cr}}
\end{equation*}

\medskip
\textbf{The solution --- a clockwise arc in the phase plane.}

Using the shifted variables $u = \vf - \Vdc/2$ and $j = \ir - \IL$,
the trajectory in the $(u,\; j\Zr)$ phase plane is a
\textbf{circle centred at the origin} with radius:
\begin{equation}
    R = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\Vdc}{2}\right)^{\!2} + (j_0\Zr)^2},
    \qquad j_0 = \ir(0^+) - \IL
    \label{eq:R_fig8}
\end{equation}
where $\ir(0^+)$ is the (negative) boost current at the instant $S_1$
opens.
Because $j_0 < 0$ (and $|j_0|$ can be large), the radius $R > \Vdc/2$.

Comparing with the upward commutation in \figref{fig:P1Fig7a}:

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Property} & \textbf{Fig.\,7 state (d) --- upward}
                  & \textbf{Fig.\,8 state (c) --- downward} \\
\midrule
Start point $(u,\;j\Zr)$
    & $(-\Vdc/2,\;i_\text{boost}\Zr)$ --- bottom right
    & $(+\Vdc/2,\;j_0\Zr)$ --- top right \\
End point $(u,\;j\Zr)$
    & $(+\Vdc/2,\;\cdot)$ --- top right
    & $(-\Vdc/2,\;\cdot)$ --- bottom left \\
Direction of travel & Counter-clockwise & \textbf{Clockwise} \\
Clamping diode & $D_1$ (clamps $\vf = \Vdc$) & $D_2$ (clamps $\vf = 0$) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\medskip
\textbf{Why $|\ir|$ peaks at $\vf = \Vdc/2$.}

From equation~\eqref{eq:swing_odes_fig8}:
$\vf$ is changing fastest when $|\ir - \IL|$ is largest.
In the phase plane, $|j| = |\ir - \IL|$ is maximum at the
\emph{bottommost point} of the clockwise trajectory, which occurs
when $u = 0$, i.e.\ when $\vf = \Vdc/2$.
Before the midpoint $|\ir|$ grows; after the midpoint $|\ir|$ shrinks.
This is the deepest point of the $\ir$ dip in the waveform.

\medskip
\textbf{What ends this state.}

As $\vf$ falls below $\Vdc/2$, the magnitude of $\ir$ starts to
decrease.
The resonant arc carries enough energy for $\vf$ to reach 0 ---
in fact, it would try to push $\vf$ \emph{below} 0 because the circle
radius $R > \Vdc/2$.
Throughout the swing $D_2$ is reverse-biased (anode at 0\,V, cathode
at $\vf > 0$).
As $\vf$ approaches 0 the reverse bias shrinks.
The instant $\vf$ tries to go fractionally below 0\,V, $D_2$ becomes
forward-biased, conducts, and clamps $\vf$ at the negative rail.
The resonant swing is arrested and the circuit enters state~(d).

% ── State (d) explanation ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection*{State (d) --- $D_2$ clamps: ZVS window opens for $S_2$}

\figentry{fig:P1Fig8e}{P1_Fig8e_state4_D2_clamp}
    {P1 Fig.\,8(e) [our state~(d)] --- $D_2$ clamps $\vf = 0$.
     $A_1$ still on; $S_2$ gated on at ZVS.
     $\ir$ ramps linearly from its negative peak back toward zero.
     Source: De~Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,5.}{0.50}

\textbf{Top panel (waveform) --- what you see in the state\,(d) strip.}

We are now in the fourth strip of the waveform in \figref{fig:P1Fig8a},
labelled \textit{D2/A1}.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\vf$ is flat at 0\,V --- clamped by $D_2$.
    \item $\ir$ is \emph{ramping upward} (toward zero) from its most
          negative value at a constant slope.
          The ramp is linear, mirroring the boost ramp of state~(b)
          but in the opposite direction --- now the energy stored in
          $\Lr$ is being returned to the DC bus.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\textbf{Bottom panel (circuit snapshot) --- $D_2$, $A_1$, and $S_2$.}

Three devices are active in \figref{fig:P1Fig8e}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{$D_2$} has just forward-biased and is clamping
          $\vf = 0$.
          Its anode (0\,V rail) is now barely above its cathode
          (pole node at $\approx 0$\,V), so it conducts.
    \item \textbf{$A_1$} remains on --- $\Lr$ is still carrying
          current and cannot be open-circuited (mandatory, same reason
          as in state~(c)).
    \item \textbf{$S_2$} is gated on at the start of this state while
          $\vf = 0$ --- zero voltage switching.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\textbf{The ZVS window --- why $S_2$ can be fired for free.}

The voltage across $S_2$ is pole-node voltage minus negative-rail
voltage:
\begin{equation*}
    V_{S_2} = \vf - 0\,\text{V} = 0 - 0 = 0\,\text{V}
\end{equation*}
$S_2$ sees exactly zero volts across it.
Firing $S_2$ costs zero switching energy: $P_\text{sw} = V_{S_2}
\times I_{S_2} = 0$.
This is the ZVS turn-on the entire commutation sequence was built to
achieve.

The window lasts as long as $D_2$ is conducting --- i.e.\ as long as
$\ir < 0$, so that D2 is still supplying current into the pole node.
The control must fire $S_2$ before $\ir$ reaches zero and the ZVS
window closes.

\medskip
\textbf{Why $\ir$ ramps back linearly.}

With $A_1$ on and $\vf = 0$ (held by $D_2$ and $S_2$), the KVL
around the auxiliary loop gives:
\begin{equation}
    \Lr\,\frac{d\ir}{dt} = \frac{\Vdc}{2} - \vf
                         = \frac{\Vdc}{2} - 0
                         = +\frac{\Vdc}{2}
    \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
    \frac{d\ir}{dt} = +\frac{\Vdc}{2\Lr}
    \label{eq:ir_rampup_fig8}
\end{equation}
The right-hand side is a positive constant --- the same magnitude as
the boost ramp of state~(b), but now driving $\ir$ \emph{upward}
toward zero.
$\ir$ therefore increases at a constant rate, recovering from its
negative peak back to zero in a straight line on the waveform.

\medskip
\textbf{What $D_2$ clamping does to the current paths.}

Before $D_2$ clamped (state~c), the discharge of $C_r$ was driven
by both $\IL$ and $|\ir|$ together.
Now $\vf$ is pinned at 0\,V and both $D_2$ (or $S_2$ once fired)
must supply all the current that is flowing out of the pole node.

KCL at the pole node:
\begin{equation}
    i_{D_2/S_2} = \IL + |\ir|
    \label{eq:stateD8_kcl}
\end{equation}
Two outward currents must be fed from the negative rail: the load
current $\IL$ to the load, and the auxiliary current $|\ir|$ flowing
through $\Lr$ toward the midpoint.
As $|\ir|$ decreases toward zero, $D_2$/$S_2$ carries progressively
less until it settles at exactly $\IL$ when $\ir = 0$.

\medskip
\textbf{Comparison with state~(e) of \figref{fig:P1Fig7a}
(paper Fig.\,7).}

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Property} & \textbf{Fig.\,7 state (e)} & \textbf{Fig.\,8 state (d)} \\
\midrule
Clamping diode        & $D_1$ at $\vf = \Vdc$ & $D_2$ at $\vf = 0$ \\
ZVS device fired      & $S_1$ (upper switch)   & $S_2$ (lower switch) \\
ZVS condition         & $\Vdc - \vf = 0$       & $\vf - 0 = 0$ \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\medskip
\textbf{What ends this state.}

$\ir$ ramps linearly toward zero.
When $\ir = 0$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The auxiliary branch carries no current.
    \item $A_1$ is gated off --- De~Doncker notes it is turned off
          \textit{``in anticipation of the current zero in order to
          minimise reverse recovery losses.''} (same phrasing as for
          $A_2$ in Fig.\,7.)
    \item $D_2$ (if still on) turns off naturally as $S_2$ now carries
          the full $\IL$.
\end{itemize}
The pole node is now in its new resting condition: $S_2$ on,
$\vf = 0$, $\ir = 0$ --- state~(e).

% ── State (e) explanation ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection*{State (e) --- $S_2$ steady: commutation complete}

\figentry{fig:P1Fig8f}{P1_Fig8f_state5_S2_steady}
    {P1 Fig.\,8(f) [our state~(e)] --- Steady lower conduction: $S_2$
     carries $\IL$, $\ir = 0$, auxiliary branch off.
     Source: De~Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,5.}{0.50}

State~(e) is the end of the downward commutation sequence.
The circuit has returned to a simple, quiet steady state --- but on
the \emph{opposite} rail from where it started.
State~(a) had $S_1$ holding the pole node to the \emph{upper} rail
with $\ir = 0$.
State~(e) has $S_2$ holding the pole node to the \emph{lower} rail
with $\ir = 0$.
The ARCP has achieved what a hard-switched inverter would have done
with a brutal voltage-step turn-off of $S_1$ --- but entirely without
switching loss.

\medskip
\textbf{Top panel (waveform) --- what you see in the state\,(e) strip.}

We are in the final strip of the waveform in \figref{fig:P1Fig8a},
labelled \textit{D2} (it is labelled for the lower rail conduction;
$S_2$ conducts in the same role).
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\vf$ is flat at 0\,V --- held by $S_2$.
    \item $\ir = 0$.
          The auxiliary inductor carries no current; the auxiliary
          branch is completely idle.
          Both waveforms are settled; nothing dynamic is happening.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\textbf{Bottom panel (circuit snapshot) --- only $S_2$ conducting.}

Compare \figref{fig:P1Fig8f} with \figref{fig:P1Fig8b} (our state~a).
They are the \emph{exact mirror images} of each other within Fig.\,8:
state~(a) had only $S_1$ bold; state~(e) has only $S_2$ bold.
Everything else --- $A_1$, $\Lr$, $D_1$, $D_2$, $S_1$ --- is off and
carries no current.
$A_1$ was gated off just before $\ir$ reached zero (state~d) and is
now fully open.
$\Lr$ is demagnetised: $\tfrac{1}{2}\Lr\ir^2 = 0$.
$S_2$ carries the full load current $\IL$ from the pole node down to
the negative DC rail.
The path is:
\[
    \text{load} \;\longrightarrow\; L_F \;\longrightarrow\;
    \text{pole node} \;\xrightarrow{\;S_2\;}\; \text{0\,V rail}
\]

The snubber capacitors are at their new settled values:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\Cr/2$ across $S_2$ --- discharged to 0\,V
          (short-circuited by the conducting $S_2$).
    \item $\Cr/2$ across $S_1$ --- charged to $\Vdc$
          (full bus voltage across the open $S_1$).
\end{itemize}
These are exactly the starting conditions for a future
\emph{upward} commutation ($S_2$ or $D_2 \to S_1$) using $A_2$ ---
the mirror-image sequence described in \figref{fig:P1Fig7a}
(paper Fig.\,7).

\medskip
\textbf{What ends this state.}

Nothing ends it from within the circuit.
State~(e) persists until the outer PWM control loop decides it is time
to switch the pole node back to the upper rail, at which point the
upward commutation sequence begins, using $A_2$ in the mirror-image
role that $A_1$ played here.
