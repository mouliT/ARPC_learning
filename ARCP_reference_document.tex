\section{Topology Evolution: From Hard-Switching to ARCP}
% ============================================================

\subsection{P1 Fig.\,1 — The Resonant Pole Inverter (RPI)}
\label{sec:P1Fig1}

% ---- PASTE FIGURE HERE after extracting PNG ----
% \figentry{fig:P1Fig1}{P1_Fig1_resonant_pole_inverter.png}
%          {The Resonant Pole Inverter (RPI). Source: De Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,1.}{0.7}

\begin{circuitbox}[Circuit Description — P1 Fig.\,1]
The RPI is the starting point of the topology evolution. Each phase leg
consists of:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Two main switches $S_1$ (upper) and $S_2$ (lower) with anti-parallel
          diodes $D_1$, $D_2$
    \item A resonant capacitor $\Cr$ placed across each switch (pole-node
          snubber)
    \item A resonant inductor $\Lr$ in series with the output, forming an LC
          filter with output capacitor $C_f$
    \item Output filter inductor $L_F$
\end{itemize}
The pole node (junction of $S_1$ and $S_2$) voltage is $\vf$.
\end{circuitbox}

\begin{statebox}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Symbol & Quantity & Typical Value \\
\midrule
$\Vdc$ & DC bus voltage & $\SI{500}{V}$ \\
$\vf$ & Pole-node voltage & $0 \leq \vf \leq \Vdc$ \\
$\ir$ & Resonant inductor current & varies \\
$I_0$ & Load/output current & $\IL$ (quasi-constant) \\
$\Lr$ & Resonant inductor & $\SI{0.33}{\micro H}$ (typical) \\
$\Cr$ & Resonant (snubber) capacitor & $\SI{1}{nF}$ (typical) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{statebox}

\begin{eqnbox}
Resonant frequency and characteristic impedance:
\begin{align}
    \omegar &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\Lr\Cr}}
    \label{eq:omega_r} \\[6pt]
    \Zr &= \sqrt{\frac{\Lr}{\Cr}}
    \label{eq:Z_r}
\end{align}
Natural commutation condition (load current charges $\Cr$ alone):
\begin{equation}
    \frac{d\vf}{dt} = \frac{\IL}{\Cr}
    \label{eq:natural_comm}
\end{equation}
\end{eqnbox}

\begin{physbox}
In the RPI, the capacitor $\Cr$ slows the rate of voltage rise/fall across the
switch, reducing turn-off losses. However, zero-voltage turn-\emph{on} is not
guaranteed — $\Cr$ may still hold residual voltage when the switch fires.
The ARCP solves this by forcing $\Cr$ to fully discharge before turn-on.
\end{physbox}

\subsection{P1 Fig.\,2 — Resonant Inverter Pole at the Load Side}
\label{sec:P1Fig2}

% \figentry{fig:P1Fig2}{P1_Fig2_resonant_pole_load_side.png}
%          {Resonant Inverter Pole at Load Side.
%           Source: De Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,1.}{0.7}

\begin{circuitbox}[Circuit Description — P1 Fig.\,2]
A variant of the RPI where the resonant inductor $\Lr$ is placed on the
\emph{load side} of the pole rather than between the DC rails.
\begin{itemize}
    \item The snubber capacitors $\Cr$ remain across each main switch
    \item $\Lr$ is in series with the load path, so it carries full load
          current continuously (unlike the ARCP auxiliary inductor)
    \item This increases conduction losses at partial load
\end{itemize}
\end{circuitbox}

\begin{physbox}
Moving $\Lr$ to the load side avoids the DC-rail placement issue of Fig.\,1
but keeps the inductor permanently in the current path.  This motivates the
\emph{auxiliary branch} idea of the ARCP (Fig.\,5): put $\Lr$ in a branch
that only conducts during commutation.
\end{physbox}

% ============================================================
\subsection{P1 Fig.\,3 — Auxiliary Resonant Diode Pole Inverter (ARDPI)}
\label{sec:P1Fig3}

% \figentry{fig:P1Fig3}{P1_Fig3_ARDPI_topology.png}
%          {Auxiliary Resonant Diode Pole Inverter (ARDPI).
%           Source: De Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,2.}{0.75}

\begin{circuitbox}[Circuit Description — P1 Fig.\,3]
The ARDPI introduces a dedicated \textbf{auxiliary branch} for commutation
assist, but uses \emph{diodes} (not controlled switches) as auxiliary devices:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Auxiliary inductor $\Lr$ is connected via diodes to the DC rails
    \item Diodes allow current to flow in one direction only, so the resonant
          current cannot be actively controlled
    \item Two circuit variants are shown side-by-side (the paper presents
          both in the same figure)
\end{itemize}
\end{circuitbox}

\begin{physbox}
The limitation of the ARDPI is that the boost magnitude cannot be controlled
— the diodes fire passively.  This means ZVS can fail at low load.
The ARCP replaces the auxiliary diodes with controlled switches ($A_1$, $A_2$)
so that the boost duration (and therefore $i_\text{boost}$) is precisely set
by the controller.
\end{physbox}

% ============================================================
\subsection{P1 Fig.\,4 — Auxiliary Resonant Pole Inverter (ARPI)}
\label{sec:P1Fig4}

% \figentry{fig:P1Fig4}{P1_Fig4_ARPI_topology.png}
%          {Auxiliary Resonant Pole Inverter (ARPI).
%           Source: De Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,2.}{0.75}

\begin{circuitbox}[Circuit Description — P1 Fig.\,4]
The ARPI replaces the ARDPI auxiliary diodes with controlled switches,
giving full control over the boost current:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Auxiliary switches (transistors) connected to DC rails via $\Lr$
    \item Controller fires the auxiliary switch for a calibrated time to
          pre-charge $\Lr$ to the required $i_\text{boost}$
    \item Still uses separate snubber caps $\Cr$ across the main devices
\end{itemize}
\end{circuitbox}

\begin{physbox}
The ARPI is the direct predecessor of the ARCP.  The ARCP refines it by
centering the auxiliary branch at the pole node (midpoint) rather than
connecting it asymmetrically — this gives identical commutation behaviour
for both the low-to-high and high-to-low transitions.
\end{physbox}

% ============================================================
\subsection{P1 Fig.\,5 — The ARCP Pole Leg (Primary Circuit)}
\label{sec:P1Fig5}

% \figentry{fig:P1Fig5}{P1_Fig5_ARCP_pole_leg.png}
%          {The Auxiliary Resonant Commutated Pole (ARCP) single phase leg.
%           Source: De Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,3.}{0.75}

\begin{circuitbox}[Circuit Description — P1 Fig.\,5]
The ARCP adds an \textbf{auxiliary resonant branch} to the RPI pole leg:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Main devices}: $S_1$, $S_2$ (IGBTs/MOSFETs) with anti-parallel
          diodes $D_1$, $D_2$
    \item \textbf{Snubber capacitors}: $\Cr$ across each main switch (forms the
          resonant capacitance at the pole node)
    \item \textbf{Auxiliary resonant inductor}: $\Lr$ — center leg, carries
          resonant current $\ir$
    \item \textbf{Auxiliary switches}: $A_1$ (connected to $+V_{dc}/2$) and
          $A_2$ (connected to $-V_{dc}/2$) — turn on briefly to initiate the
          resonant swing
    \item \textbf{Pole node}: voltage $\vf$, the state variable that must
          swing rail-to-rail before the main switch fires
\end{itemize}
The auxiliary branch ($A_1$--$\Lr$--$A_2$) is \emph{only active during
commutation}. At steady state it carries zero current.
\end{circuitbox}

\begin{statebox}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Symbol & Quantity & Role \\
\midrule
$\vf$ & Pole-node voltage & Primary state variable; must reach $\Vdc$ or 0 for ZVS \\
$\ir$ & Auxiliary inductor current & Secondary state variable; drives $\Cr$ swing \\
$\IL$ & Load current (quasi-static) & Disturbance input during commutation \\
$\Vdc$ & DC bus voltage & Forcing potential \\
$\Lr$ & Auxiliary inductance & Sets boost ramp rate and resonant frequency \\
$\Cr$ & Snubber capacitance & Sets resonant frequency and commutation speed \\
$A_1, A_2$ & Auxiliary switches & Gate-controlled; fire for $T_\text{boost}$ only \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{statebox}

\begin{eqnbox}
The two state equations governing the ARCP pole during the resonant swing
(State~(d) of the commutation sequence, see Section~\ref{sec:P1Fig7}):
\begin{align}
    \frac{d\vf}{dt} &= \frac{\ir - \IL}{2\Cr}
    \label{eq:dvf_dt} \\[6pt]
    \frac{d\ir}{dt} &= \frac{\Vdc/2 - \vf}{\Lr}
    \label{eq:dir_dt}
\end{align}
Combining~\eqref{eq:dvf_dt}--\eqref{eq:dir_dt} yields the forced oscillator:
\begin{equation}
    \ddot{\vf} + \omegar^2\,\vf = \omegar^2\cdot\frac{\Vdc}{2},
    \qquad \omegar = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\Lr\Cr}}
    \label{eq:oscillator}
\end{equation}
Define the shifted state $j = \ir - \IL$ (load current assumed constant).
Then~\eqref{eq:dvf_dt}--\eqref{eq:dir_dt} become:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{\vf} = \frac{j}{2\Cr}, \qquad
    \dot{j} = \frac{\Vdc/2 - \vf}{\Lr}
    \label{eq:shifted_states}
\end{equation}
Solution is a circle in the $(\vf,\, j)$ phase plane with centre at
$(\Vdc/2,\, 0)$ and radius $R = \sqrt{j_0^2 + (\vf_0 - \Vdc/2)^2}$.
\end{eqnbox}

\begin{physbox}
\textbf{ZVS condition:} $\vf$ must reach $\Vdc$ (or 0 for the reverse
transition) before the main switch $S_1$ (or $S_2$) fires.  This requires the
circular trajectory to reach the upper rail — i.e., the radius $R$ must satisfy
$R \geq \Vdc/2 - |\vf_0 - \Vdc/2|$.

\textbf{Role of the boost phase:} Before the resonant swing, $A_1$ is turned on
and $\ir$ ramps up linearly.  This pre-charges the inductor to give the circle
enough radius to reach the rail.  If $\IL$ is already large enough, no boost is
needed (high-current case, see Section~\ref{sec:P1Fig9}).

\textbf{Why $\Cr$ must be small:} A larger $\Cr$ stores more energy at the pole
node, requiring a larger $\ir$ to swing it.  The trade-off is: smaller $\Cr$
reduces commutation energy but increases $dv/dt$ on the main switch.
\end{physbox}

\subsection{P1 Fig.\,6 — ARCP ac-to-ac Converter (System View)}
\label{sec:P1Fig6}

% \figentry{fig:P1Fig6}{P1_Fig6_ARCP_ac_to_ac_system.png}
%          {ARCP ac-to-ac Converter — full three-phase system.
%           Source: De Doncker \& Lyons, IEEE IAS 1990, p.\,3.}{0.85}

\begin{circuitbox}[Circuit Description — P1 Fig.\,6]
The full ac-to-ac system built from ARCP pole legs:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Three pole legs (one per phase), each with the structure of Fig.\,5
    \item Each leg has its own auxiliary branch ($A_{1x}$, $A_{2x}$, $\Lr_x$,
          $\Cr_x$) — the auxiliary circuits do not interact across phases
    \item The DC bus is shared; individual phase legs commutate independently
    \item Load is a three-phase AC motor or filter
\end{itemize}
\end{circuitbox}

\begin{physbox}
This figure establishes the \textbf{scope of the ARCP}: it is a drop-in
replacement for a hard-switched inverter pole.  The control hardware must
generate one set of auxiliary gate signals per phase, fired before each
main switch transition.  The outer PWM modulator operates exactly as in a
hard-switched VSI — only the per-commutation timing sub-routine changes.
\end{physbox}

\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.35}

(c) & $A_1$ ON, $D_2$ & $\vf = 0$ & $\ir = \IL + i_\text{boost}$; boost ends, $A_1$ off \\
(d) & $\Lr$--$\Cr$ free & $\vf$ swings $0 \to \Vdc$ sinusoidally & $\ir$ follows LC circle trajectory \\
(e) & $D_1$ clamps & $\vf = \Vdc$ (clamped to upper rail) & $\ir = I_\text{peak}$; ZVS condition satisfied \\
(f) & $D_1$, $\Lr$ & $\vf = \Vdc$ (clamped) & $\ir$ ramps back down: $\dot{\ir} = -\Vdc/2\Lr$ \\

\begin{statebox}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Symbol & Meaning & Value at key instants \\
\midrule
$\vf$            & Pole-node voltage     & $0$ (states a--c) $\to \Vdc$ (states e--g) \\
$\ir$            & Auxiliary current     & $0 \to I_\text{peak} \to 0$ \\
$i_\text{boost}$ & Boost increment       & Control parameter; set by outer loop \\
$I_\text{peak}$  & Peak auxiliary current & Occurs at end of state (d) \\
$T_\text{boost}$ & Boost phase duration  & States (b)--(c) \\
$T_\text{res}$   & Resonant swing duration & State (d) only \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{statebox}

\begin{eqnbox}
\textbf{State (b) — linear boost ramp:}
\begin{equation}
    \ir(t) = \frac{\Vdc}{2\Lr}\,t, \qquad
    T_\text{boost} = \frac{2\Lr\,i_\text{boost}}{\Vdc}
    \label{eq:T_boost}
\end{equation}

\textbf{State (d) — resonant swing (forced LC oscillator):}
\begin{equation}
    \ddot{\vf} + \omegar^2\,\vf = \omegar^2\cdot\frac{\Vdc}{2},
    \qquad \omegar = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\Lr\Cr}},\quad \Zr = \sqrt{\frac{\Lr}{\Cr}}
    \label{eq:swing_ode}
\end{equation}

Solution with initial conditions $\vf(0)=0$, $\ir(0)=\IL+i_\text{boost}$:
\begin{align}
    \vf(t)  &= \frac{\Vdc}{2}(1 - \cos\omegar t)
               + (\ir(0)-\IL)\,\Zr\sin\omegar t
    \label{eq:vf_swing} \\[4pt]
    \ir(t)  &= \IL + (\ir(0)-\IL)\cos\omegar t
               + \frac{\Vdc/2}{\Zr}\sin\omegar t
    \label{eq:ir_swing}
\end{align}

\textbf{Peak current} (at end of state d, i.e.\ when $\vf = \Vdc$):
\begin{equation}
    I_\text{peak} = \IL + i_\text{boost} + \frac{\Vdc}{2\Zr}
    \label{eq:Ipeak_diode}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Resonant half-swing duration} (state d):
\begin{equation}
    T_\text{res} = \frac{\pi}{\omegar} = \pi\sqrt{\Lr\Cr}
    \label{eq:T_res}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Total commutation time} (states b through f):
\begin{equation}
    T_c = T_\text{boost} + T_\text{res}
        = \frac{2\Lr\,i_\text{boost}}{\Vdc} + \pi\sqrt{\Lr\Cr}
    \label{eq:T_comm_diode}
\end{equation}
\end{eqnbox}

\begin{physbox}
\textbf{Why seven states?}  Each state corresponds to a distinct set of
conducting devices, so the circuit topology — and therefore the governing
equation — changes at each transition.

\textbf{States (a)--(c)} are purely inductive: $\vf$ is pinned to 0 by $D_2$,
so all the DC bus voltage appears across $\Lr$ and drives a linear current ramp.
This is \emph{energy storage}, not switching.

\textbf{State (d)} is the only state where the LC oscillator is active.
The circular trajectory in the $(\vf,\, \ir-\IL)$ phase plane must reach
$\vf = \Vdc$ for ZVS to be achieved.  Radius of the circle:
$R = \sqrt{i_\text{boost}^2 + (\Vdc/2\Zr)^2}$.
ZVS requires $R \geq \Vdc/2$, which is automatically satisfied when
$i_\text{boost} \geq 0$.

\textbf{State (f)} recovers residual energy from $\Lr$ back into the DC bus
through $D_1$; this is why the auxiliary circuit is lossless in the ideal case.

\textbf{State (g)} is the new idle state.  The sequence (a)$\to$(g) is the
complete low-to-high transition; the symmetric high-to-low uses $A_2$ instead
of $A_1$ and is the mirror image.
\end{physbox}

\begin{eqnbox}
\textbf{Why $2\IL$ in the timing formula?}
When $A_1$ fires (state b), $S_2$ is still conducting: the $\Vdc/2$ rail voltage
drives $\ir$ upward at rate $\Vdc/2\Lr$.  The switch $S_2$ carries the
\emph{difference} $\IL - \ir$; it turns off naturally once $\ir = \IL$.
The boost then continues to $\IL + i_\text{boost}$, adding another
$\Lr\,i_\text{boost}/({\Vdc/2})$.  Total ramp time:
\begin{equation}
    T_\text{boost} = \frac{2\Lr(\IL + i_\text{boost})}{\Vdc}
                   + \frac{2\Lr\,\IL}{\Vdc}
                   = \frac{2\Lr(2\IL + i_\text{boost})}{\Vdc}
    \label{eq:T_boost_switch_low}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Total commutation time} (states b through e):
\begin{equation}
    T_c = \frac{2\Lr(2\IL + i_\text{boost})}{\Vdc} + \pi\sqrt{\Lr\Cr}
    \label{eq:T_comm_switch_low}
\end{equation}
\end{eqnbox}

% ---- Per-state analysis table ----

\begin{circuitbox}[State-by-State Circuit Description — P1 Fig.\,9a–c]
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.35}
\begin{tabular}{p{0.6cm} p{3.0cm} p{3.5cm} p{4.8cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{State} & \textbf{Active devices} & \textbf{Pole voltage $\vf$} & \textbf{Inductor/load current} \\
\midrule
(a) & $S_2$ conducts & $\vf = 0$ & $\ir = 0$; $\IL \geq I_{th}$; no auxiliary action \\
(b) & $C_r$ charges naturally & $\vf$ rises: $\dot{\vf} = \IL/2\Cr$ (linear) & $\ir = 0$; load current $\IL$ alone swings $\vf$ \\
(c) & $D_1$ clamps & $\vf = \Vdc$ (ZVS condition met) & $S_1$ fires at zero voltage; commutation done \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{circuitbox}

\begin{eqnbox}
Load current threshold above which no auxiliary boost is needed:
\begin{equation}
    I_{th} = \frac{2\Cr\Vdc}{T_\text{max}}
    \label{eq:Ith}
\end{equation}
When $\IL \geq I_{th}$, the load current alone charges $\Cr$ at rate
$\dot{\vf} = \IL/2\Cr$, completing the rail-to-rail swing within the
allowed commutation window $T_\text{max}$.

\textbf{Time for natural swing} (state b):
\begin{equation}
    T_\text{swing} = \frac{2\Cr\Vdc}{\IL} \leq T_\text{max}
    \qquad (\text{satisfied iff } \IL \geq I_{th})
    \label{eq:T_swing_high}
\end{equation}
\end{eqnbox}

\begin{physbox}
This is the control decision boundary.  The outer current loop compares
$|\IL|$ to $I_{th}$ on every switching cycle:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $|\IL| < I_{th}$ (low-current path, Fig.\,8): fire $A_1$ for
          duration $T_\text{boost}$; auxiliary current assists commutation.
    \item $|\IL| \geq I_{th}$ (high-current path, Fig.\,9): skip boost entirely;
          load current alone charges $\Cr$ to $\Vdc$ in time $\leq T_\text{max}$.
\end{itemize}
The three-state sequence is the fastest possible commutation: only one linear
ramp, no resonant swing, no auxiliary switch firing.  Auxiliary switch stress and
conduction losses are minimised at high load.
\end{physbox}

\begin{circuitbox}[What to Read in This Waveform — P1 Fig.\,10]
The figure shows time traces of $\vf(t)$ and $\ir(t)$ during a single
low-to-high commutation event at $\IL < I_{th}$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Trace 1} ($\ir$): starts at 0, ramps linearly during boost
          (states b--c), then follows a sinusoidal arc during the LC swing
          (state d), then ramps back down to 0 (states e--f)
    \item \textbf{Trace 2} ($\vf$): stays at 0 during the boost phase,
          then sweeps from 0 to $\Vdc$ sinusoidally, then clamps at $\Vdc$
    \item \textbf{Gate signals}: $A_1$ pulse width $= T_\text{boost}$;
          $S_1$ fires at the moment $\ir$ returns to 0 (ZVS confirmed)
\end{itemize}
\end{circuitbox}

\begin{physbox}
\textbf{How to verify ZVS from the waveform:}
$S_1$ must fire only while $\vf = \Vdc$ and while $\ir$ is decreasing
toward 0.  If $S_1$ fires before $\vf$ reaches $\Vdc$, hard-switching
occurs.  Check that the $S_1$ gate pulse begins \emph{after} $\vf$
reaches the upper rail and \emph{before} $\ir$ returns to 0.

\textbf{Key measurable time intervals:}
\begin{align*}
    T_\text{boost} &= \frac{2\Lr\,i_\text{boost}}{\Vdc}
    & &\text{(width of A}_1\text{ pulse)} \\[4pt]
    T_\text{res}   &= \pi\sqrt{\Lr\Cr}
    & &\text{(duration of } \vf \text{ swing, state d)}
\end{align*}
\end{physbox}

    \item \textbf{$\ir$ stays at 0}: the auxiliary branch is dormant
    \item \textbf{$\vf$ ramp}: $\vf$ rises linearly at rate $\IL/2\Cr$

    \item \textbf{Faster commutation}: without a boost or resonant swing,

\begin{physbox}
Compare Fig.\,10 and Fig.\,11 side by side:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Fig.\,10 (low $I$): $\vf$ swing is sinusoidal — LC oscillation
    \item Fig.\,11 (high $I$): $\vf$ swing is linear — pure capacitor charging
\end{itemize}
The crossover happens at $I_{th} = 2\Cr\Vdc/T_\text{max}$.  Above $I_{th}$,
no auxiliary energy is needed; below it, the auxiliary boost stores the
missing energy in $\Lr$ to complete the swing.
\end{physbox}
